"That was just casual discussion outside of class. It doesn't matter if you understood it or not, so don't overthink it. Getting back to the lesson, there are many ways to answer Zhou Pei's question. The Classic of Filial Piety says..."

After dropping that alarming theory on them, Ning Yi returned to the familiar territory of "Confucius says" and "the classics teach," trying to cover the proper material. But everyone's minds were already in chaos with no mood left to listen. Zhou Pei had no interest in hearing those tired old platitudes either—even if she'd listened then, she would have just thought her teacher was halfheartedly going through the motions, saying things not even as good as what she could come up with herself. Of course, she'd had no doubts about Ning Yi's learning since becoming his disciple last year.

But while talent was one thing, being a teacher with such an improper attitude was inexcusable, and influenced by Kang Xian's strict methods, Zhou Pei had been perpetually dissatisfied these days. Although she actually enjoyed his lessons and stories, this displeasure gnawed at her during class time, making her want to grab her brother and leave—a feeling that conflicted with the certain knowledge that she was truly learning something from him. These past few days the anxiety over possibly having a commandery prince consort and having to grow up had only added to her troubles, but fortunately her self-control was strong enough that she hadn't lost her composure. Instead, she'd become even more determined to correct her teacher's ways.

With the new year's semester, Ning Yi's class had actually grown to over twenty students. Zhou Pei normally excelled academically and was quite gentle with others—though the boys were too embarrassed to talk to her much, she was still well-loved and admired. This time she'd made up her mind and mobilized the masses. For this cause, she'd prepared a lengthy speech before Ning Yi arrived at class, declaring: "Even if our teacher is never serious, we ourselves must set an example." She went on about how everyone would become pillars of the nation in the future and should conduct themselves thus and so, and how they should behave in class. Honestly, Zhou Pei was quite eloquent, and besides, the atmosphere at all the academies these days was similar—teachers competed to be the most serious and stern, and someone like Ning Yi would never be allowed to teach if not for the headmaster's protection. After Zhou Pei's speech, most students thought about it and found it reasonable, deciding to impose stricter standards on themselves in class.

It was really just a matter of different perspectives. All the teachers Zhou Pei had experienced had been extremely strict—even as the little commandery princess, she'd been scolded with bulging eyes and whiskered fury, had her palms struck with the disciplinary ruler and been punished with copying the Analects. Even if not that severe, class time had to be serious at minimum, with no nonsense or jokes allowed. Ning Yi's habit of telling stories in class really rubbed her the wrong way, which had lowered her opinion of other aspects of his teaching as well.

From Ning Yi's perspective, though, these students had already become quite well-behaved under his influence. When he'd first started teaching there had been a few mischievous ones, but by now there were almost no children in the class who truly dared to act up—probably also because his reputation in the Su household was so formidable. When he actually lectured, no one dared disobey, and as for the storytelling and extending various arguments, that was meant to let everyone think for themselves—where it was interesting, where it was funny, where it was worth deep thought. Treating it like a gathering to raise a few questions and share a laugh was exactly what should happen; why would he stop it?

If Zhou Pei had enough life experience, she would have realized that after she made those proposals, most of the children agreed she was "making sense" and decided to follow her lead. They felt that lacking dignity wasn't good, that they as students should help maintain their teacher's authority. Other students, though they mentioned that Teacher had said being relaxed was better, still went along with the crowd for the time being.

Over the next few days, when Ning Yi taught, the whole group sat rigidly upright. When he occasionally cracked a joke and someone couldn't help laughing, they'd immediately force themselves back into expressions of utmost seriousness, which left Ning Yi deeply puzzled.

Naturally such self-imposed discipline couldn't last long under Ning Yi's teasing. By the end of the month, Ning Yi asked with some amusement, "Have I already become outdated? Are my jokes no longer funny?" Only then did the students speak up one after another, earnestly advising him that teachers should be stricter with them, as this would help maintain his prestige and good reputation. They all looked like little adults.

These days Ning Yi wasn't just greatly respected and feared in the Su household—his reputation for talent had spread far and wide, with people who didn't understand his personality constantly coming to visit. The students felt honored by association, but his unorthodox teaching methods were always criticized—after all, he'd only been teaching for a year, and the academy hadn't produced any notable scholars yet. When students heard others gossiping, they started thinking about their teacher's interests, and only then did they learn that Zhou Pei had used this reasoning in her speech to stir everyone up, deciding that class needed more rules.

By this point Ning Yi was stunned speechless, torn between laughter and tears, not knowing whether to laugh or be moved. When the truth came out, Zhou Pei stood up—a pretty young girl whose recent bad mood had left her with a wooden expression. As Ning Yi was walking among the students, he happened to be standing not far from her when she declared, "This student took it upon herself to act. Please punish me, Teacher"—a clear challenge from the rigorous young girl that was meant to force his hand.

Looking at her stubborn expression, Ning Yi found it amusing. After a moment, he laughed helplessly: "What's important is having your own perspective. You did very well. Why would I punish you?"

The young girl stood there facing him for a long moment, then looked at the disciplinary ruler in Ning Yi's hand that he usually waved around randomly as a pointer. She closed her eyes and held out her palm.

Though only two sentences and one action passed between them, neither had yielded an inch—a subtext the other students missed entirely. Torn between laughter and tears, Ning Yi paused for a long moment before taking the rarely-used ruler and tapping it once on her palm. Zhou Pei's tightly furrowed brows twitched slightly, but she felt no pain at all. When she opened her eyes, Ning Yi had already turned away with a smile and begun lecturing about the importance of "understanding" and "drawing inferences" in studying.

The little commandery princess pressed her lips together as she sat down without a word, not absorbing much of that lesson. Before long, others could barely detect anything wrong with her expression—she just didn't speak much all morning. At noon she sent her brother off to eat alone. Little Junwu could sense the murderous aura emanating from his sister and didn't dare approach, scurrying away dejectedly.

Wandering the academy and barely acknowledging greetings from other girls—a rare loss of composure for her—she finally sat down in the bamboo grove at the corner of the grounds. After sitting for a while in the cold, secluded grove where snow still covered the ground, her nose began to sting uncontrollably, and as tears fell, she wiped at her face and simply let herself cry.

She didn't really understand why she was suddenly crying so hard. Normally she wouldn't get angry over such things—her teacher was a capable person, and she understood in her heart that his teaching methods weren't necessarily useless. Actually, these past days whenever she thought about how her father the prince wanted to select a husband for her, her heart would race and then feel hollow.

After more than a decade of life, just when she was starting to become sensible, she had to get married¹, and all those things she'd wanted to do—she'd never accomplished any of them.

If she were a few years older, she might find the distress she felt now truly childish, but at this moment, the thirteen-year-old girl could only sit by the bamboo grove crying like pear blossoms in the rain³, her eyes hazy with tears.

Just as she was grieving and wiping away tears, she noticed a figure standing not far away. Hurriedly wiping her eyes, she looked over and saw it was Ning Yi, preparing to leave after finishing his teaching. Her vision, blurred by tears, had prevented her from noticing him as he walked past, and he hadn't seen her either, so it was only in this moment that they saw each other clearly. The thirteen-year-old girl desperately tried to compose her face and stop the tears, but for a moment she couldn't stop them no matter how hard she tried...

******************

The afternoon sun was bright. Though it still held no warmth, compared to overcast days it at least lifted one's spirits more.

Having endured a season of bitter winter, tender grass had sprouted in the small courtyard. Since the previous owner had no plans or desire to maintain the yard, tender grass now grew across the ground like a carpet, and in some places unmelted snow piles added to the atmosphere of vibrant life. As wind chimes tinkled softly under the eaves, a girl in an elegant white and green dress sat on the railing eating a meat roll.

By the standards of this era, the girl's dress and bearing already reached the threshold of adulthood, but in reality, no matter how dignified her appearance or how serious her manner, a girl only about 1.3 meters tall still looked like a little thing who hadn't grown up yet. Her petite frame combined with the expression of someone trying hard to look mature, plus her expensive and refined dress and accessories, made her seem more like a porcelain doll earnestly trying to grow up.

After being seen crying by her teacher, a tangle of embarrassment, sadness, and anxiety left Zhou Pei unable to name her own mood. While she'd always found Ning Yi's teaching style amusing despite her complaints, her recent intolerance stemmed from her own troubled feelings, and the failure of her classroom reform had caused small frustrations to pile up in her heart.

Of course she couldn't possibly admit any of this out loud. She'd been hoping her teacher would convince her, and while the crying incident couldn't be mentioned, there was also no way to pretend it hadn't happened, so she'd followed Ning Yi all the way here. She hadn't eaten at noon and bought a meat roll to gnaw on as she walked.

But she naturally couldn't understand Ning Yi's thoughts. While he didn't approve of girls marrying at thirteen or fourteen, this was the Wu Dynasty's norm—the laws and customs were immutable, and since she wasn't his daughter, speaking out would be useless. Zhou Pei probably felt she was already an adult, but in reality she was still a child, and he didn't want to push her toward premature maturity or humor her with childish tricks. Life's matters could only be understood and accepted on her own, and though she was troubled now, once married she'd naturally come to accept it.

The young girl sat under the eaves waiting but received no guidance from Ning Yi. Thinking her teacher was inside doing some experiment, she viciously bit into her meat roll several times. Then she saw the door open and Ning Yi emerge carrying a long bundle on his back. He asked, "You and Junwu are still playing at the academy this afternoon?"

Zhou Pei looked at the long bundle on his back, swallowed the food in her mouth, and bit her lip: "Where is Teacher going?"

"To the Imperial Son-in-law's Mansion to see if your Uncle Lu is there. You should head back to the academy."

"Going to see Grandfather the Imperial Son-in-law... then I'm going too."

Zhou Pei thought for a moment, then lifted her skirt hem and stood up to follow behind him. She glanced at the bundle on Ning Yi's back—it wasn't wrapped carefully, with a bamboo tube sticking out from one corner. She and Junwu had seen this thing when they came over before, and though the teacher wouldn't let them touch it, they knew it was a fire lance² from the military.

Since she was more interested in real matters than in "playing" at the academy, and because the gloom of the past few days remained unresolved, she felt she had to follow him. If her teacher brought it up, she'd have to argue with him to let him know she wasn't some little kid who only knew how to cry—him seeing that earlier had been purely an accident. This was what mattered most.

FOOTNOTES:

¹ *In Tang and Song Dynasty China (which this fictional Wu Dynasty mirrors), aristocratic girls typically married between ages 13-16. This was considered normal and appropriate for the period. The protagonist Ning Yi is a transmigrator from modern times and privately disagrees with this practice, but recognizes he cannot change established social customs that don't directly involve his own family.*

² *The fire lance (突火枪 tuhuoqiang) was an early gunpowder weapon used in Song Dynasty China, consisting of a bamboo or metal tube attached to a spear shaft. It fired projectiles using gunpowder charges and was a predecessor to firearms.*

³ *Classical poetic expression (梨花带雨 lihua daiyu) from Tang Dynasty poetry, comparing a tearful face to white pear blossoms wet with rain—evoking delicate beauty in sorrow.*
