Liao dogs daring to trespass into the Wu Dynasty's heartland to commit murder and assassination—in Jiangning these days, there was probably only one event that matched this description: the assassination attempt at Zhu's a few days earlier. For the sake of Zhu's second branch's reputation, Ning Yi had spent the past several days using Lu Agui's connections to spread word of the incident far and wide, stirring up quite the storm. He himself had been continuously following leads on the whereabouts of those two escaped assassins, though so far there had been no new developments. But from what these three men were saying, something new seemed to have happened last night.

Sitting nearby, he listened patiently for a while. The three men mostly talked about how formidable that leading Liao warrior was, how impossibly fast his blade moved, how one should dodge such attacks. Xiao Chan listened for a few moments before quietly asking Ning Yi, "Young Master, are they talking about what happened to Old Master Qin?" Ning Yi nodded slightly, then walked toward their table. "Greetings, gentlemen."

The three men, seeing that the person approaching was a scholarly-looking young man, couldn't help but pause in surprise. Then they heard Ning Yi say, "Just now I overheard you discussing the Liao incident, which seems related to the assassination attempt several days ago. I've also heard about that matter, but I don't know what happened last night. You three clearly participated in the events. They say that 'the greatest heroes serve the nation and its people'—I've always admired heroic men of valor like yourselves. Therefore, I would like to hear about last night's events, and humbly request your instruction in this matter."

After finishing this greeting, he added, "Oh, my name is Ning Liheng. I've also practiced some martial arts myself. In the jianghu, people bestowed upon me the nickname Blood Hand Butcher.¹ Honored to meet you."

Following behind him, Xiao Chan had been deeply impressed by how naturally and smoothly her young master delivered that string of jianghu pleasantries. But when he mentioned "Blood Hand Butcher," her expression twitched slightly. The little maid had always assumed this was a nickname her young master invented to inflate his own reputation—harmless boasting within the household, where everyone even played along with it. Now watching him so shamelessly deceive these jianghu men with it, she felt herself starting to crack.

The three jianghu men shared a similar reaction. At first, hearing Ning Yi speak so respectfully, they'd taken him for a patriotic young scholar full of righteous passion. But when he suddenly claimed people had bestowed upon him the nickname Blood Hand Butcher, they could only exchange bewildered glances before managing responses like "Long admired your reputation" and "Honored to meet you."

"My name is Xiong Mo."

"I'm Lin Jinquan."

"Zhao Xing, at your service."

Interestingly, none of them offered any impressive nicknames of their own—perhaps because standing alongside a bit-part moniker like "Blood Hand Butcher" would lower their own standing.

Apart from the awkward nickname, Ning Yi actually proved quite socially adept in other respects. He ordered the finest and most expensive breakfast spread the establishment offered, had Xiao Chan cover the bill, and only then listened as the three men explained what had happened last night.

The Liao assassination attempt in Jiangning had already been widely publicized by Ning Yi's propaganda efforts, which not only boosted Brocade Restaurant's fame but also significantly inflamed the patriotic fervor of local jianghu warriors. Over the past few days, while the authorities searched the city for the two escaped Liao assassins, they'd also posted a bounty of nearly a thousand taels of silver, prompting jianghu people to network and coordinate with each other—motivated by money, by fame, and naturally by patriotic passion—all hoping to capture the two Liao men.

While everyone conducted this citywide manhunt, the unfortunate young nobleman whom the vicious Jin'er had practically turned his lower body into a pincushion with her hairpin managed to barely survive, largely because Jin'er ultimately hadn't dared actually kill him and unconsciously held back once she'd stabbed him enough. Somehow—though exactly how remained unclear given the supposedly secure detention arrangements—his holding location was infiltrated last night by four men who rescued the young nobleman and fought their way out, ultimately proving impossible to stop despite the resistance.

Ning Yi hadn't known about this development, but the jianghu people who'd been monitoring the situation received word immediately and rushed out of the city to pursue and intercept, coordinated with official troops. However, these Liao warriors moved with incredible speed and possessed extremely high martial skills, preventing the ragtag coalition of jianghu fighters and government constables from forming an effective encirclement in the mountainous terrain around Jiangning. After several skirmishes of varying scales during the pursuit through the mountains, they managed to take down only one warrior, while the other three escaped with the half-dead young nobleman.

The fact that such an incident could occur even on Jiangning's home territory left Ning Yi somewhat stunned, but according to these three men, the most martially skilled among the escaped group wasn't the tall thin man or the burly fellow with the eye bandaged over half his face—there was another warrior whose abilities were truly terrifying. This man had a solid, dark-skinned build and was covered head to toe in scars, looking like some kind of fearsome demon god, and it was primarily because he carved out a bloody path that they'd had any chance of escaping at all. Of course, the thin man and the one-eyed giant were no pushovers either—the group quickly recognized these were likely Liao's most elite soldiers, the type who'd survived countless battlefield slaughters.

"From daily martial training, I'd thought I'd achieved some real skill," Zhao Xing said, gesturing to his injured shoulder, "but that scar-covered warrior truly left me shaken just thinking about him. Last night when his blade came down, I blocked with everything I had and still got sent flying several meters. This right shoulder got wrenched open—probably won't be able to lift anything for quite a while. My back slammed into something and it still hurts now. If they hadn't been desperate to escape and had given me just one more strike, I'd probably have died right there."

Xiong Mo considered this thoughtfully. "That man might have cultivated some truly superior internal cultivation method." Lin Jinquan nodded in agreement, and Ning Yi chimed in with appropriate praise: "Regardless, your heroic righteousness is genuinely admirable."

Through this conversation, Ning Yi finally grasped the full picture of events. It turned out that beyond the five assassins, there were actually two additional expert warriors who hadn't participated in the original attack. Even more concerning, these outsiders had somehow managed to infiltrate the detention facility where the young nobleman was being held—suggesting their influence extended well beyond just this handful of fighters. These three men probably didn't qualify as truly formidable martial artists by Lu Hongti's standards—likely just ordinary cannon fodder in her eyes—but the scar-covered "black demon god" they described must be a genuine expert, capable of sending a martial artist flying with a single blade strike, which clearly required superior internal power technique. He wondered how such a warrior would compare to Lu Hongti's abilities.

Various questions circled through his mind as these jianghu men prepared to leave, at which point he had Xiao Chan catch up with them to present gift boxes of food and some banknotes. While they say scholars live poorly and martial artists need wealth,² those actually making their living by the blade's edge in the jianghu probably didn't live particularly well. Since everyone had coincidentally ended up on the same side in this affair, Ning Yi was quite willing to provide what modest help he could. Xiao Chan spoke with such tactful courtesy that it took considerable time before the three jianghu men finally accepted the gifts.

That afternoon after classes ended, Ning Yi intended to find Lu Agui and inquire about last night's events. He wasn't truly that passionate about patriotism—it was simply that he'd been the one to stop the assassins targeting Old Qin, and even though the likelihood of them seeking revenge remained small, he'd gotten involved regardless, so understanding the situation as thoroughly as possible seemed worthwhile. However, when he arrived at the Imperial Son-in-law's Mansion that afternoon, Lu Agui happened to be out, and Kang Xian wasn't home either, so he could only turn back, planning to ask again in a few days since the matter wasn't particularly urgent.

Walking back toward the Su residence through several streets, he didn't notice the figure trailing at a distance behind him—Chen Feng, the constable from Jiangning Prefecture, who glanced back questioningly toward the Imperial Son-in-law's Mansion before continuing to follow.

After turning through two more streets, Chen Feng suddenly sensed a watching gaze, and in that instant of realization, a hand seized his shoulder. Though his martial arts were decent and he immediately tried to counter, the opponent's strength was so overwhelming he couldn't break free before being spun around at the street corner. Getting a clear look, he finally relaxed—this person was actually someone he knew.

The man who'd intercepted Chen Feng was none other than Lu Agui, currently serving as manager at the Imperial Son-in-law's Mansion. Ning Yi had been returning from the mansion while Lu Agui was heading back toward it, and spotting Ning Yi, he'd originally intended to call out a greeting—only to notice the tail following behind. Since he happened to recognize this particular tail, he'd abandoned the greeting idea and intercepted the pursuer instead.

"Constable Chen, you've certainly got free time lately—instead of catching those vicious criminals, you're playing at surveillance games. As far as I know, that young gentleman ahead is a law-abiding citizen who just recently prevented a Liao assassination attempt. What crime has he committed?"

Chen Feng frowned. "Whether someone's committed a crime gets decided by the authorities, not by you or me. I know he seems to have some connection with your people, Lu Agui—are you planning to interfere?"

"I wouldn't call it interfering—I just want to know what kind of trouble this involves. Lord Ming and this young man are close friends, and I'd like to understand the situation."

"...After all this time, you've truly become that Imperial Son-in-law's lapdog..."

"Lord Ming saved my life—I owe him my death in return... You used to be able to say working for an Imperial Son-in-law with no real achievements was pointless, but you certainly can't say that anymore. This Young Master Ning isn't as simple as he appears. The favor he's recently bestowed on all under Heaven exceeds anything you can imagine, and he just saved Qin Siyuan as well. If he's in some kind of trouble, certain people will risk everything to protect him. And I guarantee you he's a good man. So what's the problem here? Why don't we go somewhere nearby to talk this over and see if the Imperial Son-in-law's Mansion can help resolve it?"

"All under Heaven? Favor?" Chen Feng's eyes showed a flash of confusion. "I know he has some literary reputation in Jiangning, being young and talented, but doesn't that seem rather excessive praise?"

Lu Agui thought for a moment, then smiled oddly. "It's not something you can imagine... So how about it? Find a place for tea and catch up on old times?"

After a long silence, Chen Feng looked at him and finally spoke. "...Fine."

As the sky darkened, a bright waxing moon rose overhead.

At the Imperial Son-in-law's Mansion, Zhou Pei had just finished dinner and emerged from the Princess's quarters, walking through the lantern-lit courtyards on her way to request instruction on some scholarly matters from the Imperial Son-in-law, as was her usual custom.

She spent more than half her time living at this mansion, and naturally none of the household servants or guards would stop her wherever she went. Approaching the Imperial Son-in-law's study, she heard voices coming from inside.

"A constable? Why would a constable be watching Liheng?" This was the Imperial Son-in-law's voice. "Has something happened?"

She could occasionally overhear the Imperial Son-in-law discussing various significant matters here—perhaps confidential ones. Sometimes Zhou Pei would listen, other times she'd turn and leave, adhering to the principle that eavesdropping was improper. But today's topic made her decide to listen a while longer, so she settled down beneath the eaves...

FOOTNOTES:

¹ In jianghu culture, martial artists' nicknames are traditionally bestowed by others based on their deeds and reputation. Self-proclaimed nicknames are considered shameful. "Blood Hand Butcher" sounds menacing but is generic enough to be what jianghu people call a "bit part nickname"—the kind given to minor characters in stories. Grandiose names like this often imply a low-level or inexperienced individual trying to sound impressive. The three men likely have more prestigious nicknames but don't share them, as doing so alongside such a generic moniker would lower their standing by association.

² "穷文富武" (poor scholars, wealthy martial artists) is a traditional saying suggesting martial arts training brings wealth while scholarly pursuits bring poverty. However, the reality was that most jianghu wanderers lived in desperate poverty despite their skills, making their living through dangerous work—metaphorically described as "licking blood from the blade edge" (刀口舔血).
