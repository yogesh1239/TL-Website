Starting after dinner, visitors came and went in a steady stream throughout the evening. Ning Yi hadn't wanted to attract this kind of attention over the afternoon's incident, but during the fight he'd inevitably taken a punch or two, and his legs had sustained some injuries as well. The problems weren't serious—he'd already applied medicine and bandaged everything at Zhu's—but the medicinal smell couldn't be hidden, and once Xiao Chan heard that he'd been involved in something dangerous and gotten hurt in what sounded like an extremely perilous situation, she tearfully insisted on checking his wounds herself.

"Young Master always likes those dangerous things... last year with that assassin too... your hand just finished healing from the burn... and now this again..."

Having passed the New Year, Xiao Chan had entered her seventeenth year, yet this petite and adorable young maid didn't put much stock in her young master's fearsome reputation as the "Bloody Butcher." Though Ning Yi exercised every day and occasionally bragged to the household about being invincible under heaven, and even had the proud achievement of taking down a fierce bandit with a fire lance before New Year's, in Xiao Chan's heart her young master was still fundamentally a frail scholar. After she fussed over his injuries for a while, Su Tan'er returned with Xing'er and Juan'er, then had Xing'er summon a doctor, and before nightfall the news that Ning Yi had been injured outside spread throughout the Su mansion.

The incident that had occurred at Zhu's that afternoon had already stirred up quite a commotion throughout Jiangning, and within just half a day, several people in the Su mansion had caught wind of different versions. The version circulating tended toward the indignant side, telling a passionate story about how Liao people had attempted to assassinate a court official at Zhu's restaurant, only to be stopped by the crowd—though this was actually the result of arrangements Ning Yi had made afterward, coordinated with forces from Prince Kang's mansion and the Imperial Son-in-law's mansion.

That sudden intervention in the afternoon had stemmed from the need to save Qin Siyuan. If possible at the time, Ning Yi wouldn't have wanted the fight to break out inside Zhu's—after all, business was booming with so many customers, and if people suffered an unprovoked disaster like that, who would dare come back? Unfortunately, that group's determination to attack had been resolute, and in the end there'd been no avoiding what happened.

Calculating afterward, over thirty people had sustained light or serious injuries, though fortunately no innocent customers had died, which could already be considered extremely fortunate. After roughly understanding the situation, Ning Yi disbursed a substantial sum from the shop's funds for compensation—though he didn't actually use the word "compensation," instead framing it as rewards for everyone who'd shown bravery in stopping the Liao people, with even those who hadn't been injured but had been present in the hall receiving a sum of money.

That's what he said, but who actually knew those men were Liao people at the time? The assassins had fought ferociously with excellent martial skills, and none of the customers had dared engage them—everyone had been too busy dodging to do anything else. However, after these arrangements were made, Ning Yi had Lu Agui organize some people to help spread certain rumors and propaganda, mainly emphasizing how arrogantly the Liao people had behaved, bullying the Central Plains as if we had no one capable of standing up to them, daring to barge right into Jiangning to commit assassination, only to be met with a mass uprising of Wu Dynasty citizens who, despite the enemy's ferocity, showed that we Wu Dynasty people weren't to be trifled with either, courageously throwing themselves into the fray without regard for their own safety and so forth... The main goal was to spin this into a praiseworthy heroic event, stirring up everyone's patriotic sentiments, even embellishing accounts of the injured and calling them heroes, with plans to feature this heavily in the shop's promotions over the next month—who knew, maybe they could actually turn this disaster into an opportunity.

In any case, this was all the crisis management he could do, and Ning Yi was quite practiced at it, so with cooperation from forces under Kang Xian's command, there shouldn't be any major problems.

Regarding the afternoon's events, Ning Yi still found certain things rather strange. Though these five men had decent skills, the assassination itself had been somewhat mindless, clearly not the result of careful planning, which suggested there wasn't an organized group directing them from behind. Besides, with Liao currently seeking aid from Wu, they had no reason to go through the trouble of killing Qin Siyuan—it simply made no sense. Later, combining evidence from items found on the young nobleman's person with discussions involving Old Qin, Lu Agui and others, they'd pieced together a rather absurd conclusion.

This leading young nobleman was most likely a minor Liao aristocrat who'd brought his formidable household guards south under the pretense of trade, traveling from place to place. With Jin-Liao relations currently tense and Liao holding their frustration inside while Wu public opinion clamored for taking advantage of the situation to attack Liao, he'd probably heard rumors about Old Qin as well, and upon arriving in Jiangning and encountering him by chance, had angrily decided to carry out the assassination.

This was the most likely speculation, though whether it was actually true would require further investigation to confirm. Two members of that group had escaped, and government forces were still conducting searches—if they couldn't be caught, Ning Yi would find it rather troublesome. Throughout history, fanatics had always been the hardest to deal with, and passionate patriots who could actually take action were precisely one such type. Right now he could only hope that only the leading nobleman had been a Liao hothead, with the rest merely following orders, because otherwise if they decided to seek revenge, while he personally wasn't worried, he feared for Zhu's.

As for that gang of thugs who'd happened to show up at the wrong time, Ning Yi couldn't be bothered to worry about them—Kang Xian, Lu Agui, and even Song Qian wouldn't let this matter slide easily, and after experiencing this incident and saving Qin Siyuan's life, Song Qian would likely receive rewards thanks to Kang Xian's gratitude, which meant those thugs and whoever stood behind them were definitely in for some bad luck...

After sending off the stream of visitors one by one, Ning Yi roughly recounted the afternoon's detailed sequence of events, though he didn't mention killing two people himself, mainly just talking about Old Qin, with the fighting parts recounted in an embellished, laughing manner.

"...What happened next, I can describe with the phrase 'sudden reversal'¹, because all of a sudden... a Bull Demon King came charging out..."

After joking around like this for a while, when they finally went to bed late at night, Su Tan'er lay on his chest with her long hair spread loosely around her, seeming to sniff the medicinal scent from the bandages on his chest before speaking softly: "It was actually quite dangerous this afternoon, wasn't it?"

"Mm?"

Since returning home that evening after hearing Ning Yi roughly explain his injuries, Su Tan'er had been rather quiet, just staying by Ning Yi's side the whole time, helping when Xiao Chan brought face cloths and tea water, though it wasn't clear why she was in this particular mood. Only now did she say: "Before coming home, I'd already heard about the Liao assassination incident, but I didn't realize my husband had been there too. They said the assassins were extremely fierce—two died, one was seriously injured, and two escaped with their eyes blinded. Dozens of people were injured in that tavern, and someone fired a gun... They said dozens of people all helped courageously subdue the criminals, but things are never that simple..."

She paused slightly, both of them currently nestled under the covers with Su Tan'er wearing only her dudou², pressed against Ning Yi, her tone very soft: "They said a scholar fired the gun, then fought hand-to-hand with criminals wielding blades and clubs... thinking about all the death and injury makes it seem so dangerous. I didn't really process it at the time, but after coming back and seeing you, I started to imagine it... and I got scared."

Ning Yi paused slightly, his hand stopping its movement on her back: "Uh... actually it wasn't that bad..." In truth, for him, that fight had been extremely dangerous.

But Su Tan'er didn't say anything more about it, simply rubbing her cheek against the bandages a few times before closing her eyes: "Mm."

The room lay quiet and dark, and after a while longer, Su Tan'er opened her eyes again, speaking with a soft laugh: "My husband should find time... to take Xiao Chan as a concubine³."

"Eh?"

"I don't care for you enough..." Su Tan'er said softly. "I know it must have been very dangerous, that my husband shouldn't have rushed into such matters casually, but then I thought about how if my husband encountered Master Qin in trouble, you would definitely rush out to help, and as a woman I shouldn't say anything about that⁴, so I found myself unable to say anything, even though... my heart is still worried, and thinking about it makes me scared..."

She tilted her face slightly, smiling: "If my husband takes Little Chan as a concubine, I can have her constantly nagging at you, when really it's my own instruction, hehe..."

Ning Yi fell silent for a moment, somewhat at a loss, and could only stroke her spine while sighing: "You'd have to refer to yourself as 'this concubine' for that to sound truly virtuous..."

In fact, Su Tan'er basically had no problems with virtuousness, and most of the time she naturally used terms like "this concubine"⁵ to refer to herself, though sometimes using "I" was something Ning Yi had influenced her toward.

In the past when the two of them met every few days on the second-floor terrace across the way, they'd occasionally use rather uninhibited language, with Ning Yi often adopting a friend's attitude to remark "Su Tan'er, you're quite cunning..." and Su Tan'er had tried responding with a friend's attitude as well, and when she was in a good mood, they'd sometimes engaged in exchanges during their meetings like "Who are you, brother, and why have you come to my balcony?" "Your husband." "...A pleasure to meet you, a pleasure indeed." Now that their relationship had reached its most intimate stage, the previous uninhibited banter had naturally transformed into a kind of private pleasure, with Su Tan'er only occasionally adopting a not-quite-so-ladylike posture during their private intimate moments, though if anyone else were present, even just maids like Xiao Chan, Juan'er and Xing'er, she would never behave this way.

At this point Su Tan'er nodded: "This concubine was wrong..." After a while, she put away her playful mood: "Actually my husband likes Little Chan too, doesn't he? Little Chan does like you, though I don't know if, uh..."

This was the first time the two of them had discussed Xiao Chan after consummating their marriage, with certain matters probably being mutually understood between them. Regarding the possibility that Ning Yi might later take Xiao Chan as a concubine, Su Tan'er wasn't unprepared—she'd even actively arranged for Xiao Chan to keep Ning Yi company before. Only now, discussing these things after consummating their marriage, her feelings were inevitably complex, since she still wasn't clear whether Ning Yi and Xiao Chan had actually had relations or not. If they had and she'd kept silent all this time, wouldn't she be failing this maid who was like a sister to her?

She was someone who saw matters extremely clearly, and choosing this evening to finally bring up Xiao Chan's situation, Ning Yi thought it over before selectively explaining the key points of his relationship with Xiao Chan—how as a maid, it wouldn't be appropriate for Xiao Chan to become pregnant before her mistress, while abortion medicine would harm her body, so Ning Yi had been concerned about that and thus hadn't consummated things with her. Ning Yi had roughly known before about Su Tan'er instructing Xiao Chan in this matter, and mentioning it now with a smile, Su Tan'er laughed somewhat bashfully and nestled against Ning Yi without saying anything more.

The rest of the night passed without further conversation. The next morning, Ning Yi rose early again. Though his body bore injuries, under the effects of excellent internal energy cultivation and medicine, he already felt little discomfort. The martial art Lu Hongti had taught him was meant to be practiced during ordinary movement anyway, with excellent health-strengthening effects, only becoming somewhat harmful to the body when force was suddenly exerted. After doing some warm-up exercises in the courtyard, he jogged away from the Su mansion, running through the morning mist along the Qinhuai River toward the small building where Yunzhu lived.

No matter what, Yunzhu had been injured yesterday, and with Yuan Jin'er jumping into the river in such cold weather, he was inevitably somewhat worried about them both...

FOOTNOTES:

¹ 峰回路转 (fēng huí lù zhuǎn) is a classical Chinese idiom literally meaning "the peak turns and the path winds," describing an unexpected positive development or dramatic reversal in a difficult situation. The phrase evokes mountain paths that suddenly reveal new vistas, suggesting hope emerging from apparent dead ends.

² Dudou is a traditional Chinese undergarment, a diamond-shaped piece of fabric that ties around the neck and waist, covering the chest and abdomen. Similar to a halter top, it was the standard intimate garment for women in ancient China, worn under outer robes.

³ In traditional Chinese households, the wife had significant authority to select and approve concubines for her husband, often choosing trusted maids from her own household. The formal "taking" of a concubine (收房, shōu fáng) involved both official recognition of her elevated status within the household hierarchy and consummation of the relationship. Cultural hierarchy strictly dictated that maids could not become pregnant before the mistress, making the timing of consummation a delicate and strategic matter requiring the wife's approval and coordination.

⁴ Traditional wifely propriety (妇道人家, fù dào rén jiā) imposed strict constraints forbidding wives from attempting to control or directly criticize their husband's choices, even when those choices were dangerous or potentially harmful. A proper wife was expected to support her husband's decisions without complaint or interference, regardless of her personal fears or objections. Su Tan'er's suggestion to use Xiao Chan as a concubine is a clever circumvention of this cultural restriction—a concubine could "nag" about safety concerns and express worries in ways that a proper wife could not, allowing Su Tan'er to indirectly protect her husband while maintaining her socially expected demeanor.

⁵ 妾身 (qiè shēn) is the traditional humble self-reference used by wives, literally meaning "this concubine" or "your servant." Despite being the legal wife, a woman would use this self-effacing term when speaking to or about her husband, reflecting Confucian ideals of wifely humility and deference. Su Tan'er's occasional use of the casual 我 (wǒ, "I") instead shows Ning Yi's modernizing influence on her speech patterns, reflecting the growing intimacy and equality in their relationship.
