"Get out of the way! Stop pushing..."

"Shh, keep it down."

"What's the big deal? They can't hear us from way over there..."

Scattered whispers drifted through the air as a boy and young girl crouched behind a pillar along the third-floor corridor's eaves, peering down at the street below in the afternoon light.

This stretch of streets in the eastern district was one of Jiangning's more leisurely quarters—perfect for relaxation, though lacking the abundance of pleasure houses found elsewhere, instead hosting countless teahouses and taverns whose flying eaves and interlocking bracket sets* created an intricate architectural tapestry above the narrow streets below. Vendors selling various specialty pastries regularly gathered there, while the taverns and teahouses frequently attracted opera singers and storytellers, creating a constant hum of entertainment that made the area particularly popular with those seeking cultured diversions.

The Fragrant Warmth Tea House where they now hid was the largest and most renowned establishment in the vicinity, its third floor offering panoramic views of the surrounding teahouse rooftops and street scenes below, though the third floor consisted entirely of private rooms with correspondingly high prices. The two children currently positioned at the corridor's railing wore luxurious clothing that marked them as far above ordinary merchant families in both quality and bearing, their current furtive behavior suggesting they'd discovered something particularly intriguing worth this secretive observation.

"Ahhhh... he's actually pouring tea for someone! He definitely isn't saying anything good..."

"That guy's face looks so strange—turning red one moment, white the next..."

"He's a total smiling tiger,¹ putting on that fake act again. The Wu family guy must be furious..."

"Sister, Teacher often makes you angry too... okay, okay, I won't say anymore, I was wrong..."

The young girl shot the boy a fierce glare that immediately silenced his commentary.

Across the street, two men were chatting over tea by a second-floor window in the Respectful Bamboo Grove teahouse. After one of them departed, the other began to read and write, prompting the observing siblings to debate whether they should run over and greet him.

"Maybe he's writing something interesting..." the little boy mused, chin propped on his hands as he leaned against the railing.

Before the girl could respond, a voice spoke from behind them: "Oh? Who might that be?"

Both children spun around to find several people had appeared behind them without making a sound. The man who'd spoken was approximately forty years old with a square face and a nearly three-inch beard that lent him an air of dignified authority tempered by prosperity, his expression carrying a pleasant smile. When faced with his inquiry, the girl named Zhou Pei blinked rapidly, her rolling eyes suggesting reluctance to tell the truth, while the boy called Zhou Junwu suddenly pressed his lips together and began shaking his head vigorously. The middle-aged man's smile faltered slightly at their obvious evasiveness.

"Oh, you cannot say..."

"No, we don't know him," Junwu said with a toothy grin, causing his sister's wildly rolling gaze to freeze. The middle-aged man nodded with an "oh" of understanding before glancing once more toward the teahouse across the way. "However, your father sees that this person is young and has fine bearing—I would quite like to make his acquaintance." At this point, something seemed to occur to him, and he bent down to whisper in the young girl's ear in tones only she could hear: "Speaking of which, Pei'er, you'll be of marriageable age² in just two years. All those talented young men were gathered for conversation, yet you refused to join them. Instead, you're hiding out here with Junwu..."

"How could that be possible!" Zhou Pei burst out, then froze in realization before turning her face away in embarrassment, slapping her forehead in frustration. The middle-aged man chuckled before turning to his accompanying entourage: "In that case, we truly have no way of knowing who this person is. Do any of you gentlemen happen to recognize him?"

"In response to Your Highness, this person is the son-in-law of the Su household—Ning Yi, styled Liheng," replied one of the accompanying elderly men, approximately fifty years old, with a smile and respectful bow.

"Oh? The foremost talent?" The man addressed as 'Your Highness' looked surprised, his gaze shifting from the bandaged, remarkably young figure across the way—whose youth seemed at odds with his reputation—to the children beside him, his eyes a mixture of dawning understanding and continued puzzlement.

"I have long heard that this person possesses remarkable poetic talent, though I have never had the opportunity to witness it myself. Everyone present here are learned scholars—does anyone happen to be acquainted with him..." He had intended to suggest that if so, perhaps an introduction could be arranged for his son, but noticing the children's attitudes, he reconsidered his approach. "I wonder... does this person's reputation match reality?"

This middle-aged man was indeed Zhou Pei and Zhou Junwu's father, one of the imperial family members* residing in Jiangning—Prince Kang, Zhou Yong. Though he bore the title of prince, he had achieved little of note and lacked the money-making abilities of his aunt Zhou Xuan and her husband Kang Xian. He possessed no particular aptitude for poetry or literature, preferring to spend his days wandering about listening to opera, engaging in activities like cockfighting and dog racing, and occasionally hunting where he might successfully shoot a rabbit or two.

Naturally, literary pursuits remained a form of entertainment appreciated by all social classes, and he would periodically indulge in cultured pretensions. With his status, whenever he desired sophistication, sophistication would inevitably present itself, and the several men following him now represented some of Jiangning's most notable talents. When he posed his question, one of them stepped forward with a cupped-hand salute: "Ning Yi does indeed possess considerable talent." These were kind words, and if any of the Su family's wastrel sons had been present, they would have been shocked to see the speaker was Liu Qingdi, with whom there had been previous conflicts. However, after speaking these words, he smiled and continued: "Only recently, ah... heh, this matter bears no relation to literature, so perhaps it's better left unsaid. Ning Yi's poetic talent has always earned my admiration."

"Oh? Has something happened to this Ning Yi? Qingdi, please do tell us—everyone would like to hear..." Zhou Yong gazed at him with a smiling expression.

Liu Qingdi's expression shifted through various emotions as he hesitated for quite some time before finally nodding: "Since that is the case, then... actually, this is hardly fresh news, though Your Highness the Prince may not have heard it yet. The matter dates back approximately two months when an incident occurred at the Su family cloth merchants..."

Over these past two months, the struggle that had unfolded within Jiangning's cloth trade possessed genuine dramatic appeal: first the Su family's assassination attempt, which was exposed as a trap set by certain rivals, followed by the Su family's remarkable recovery from their declining position to mount an impressive campaign for imperial merchant status, only to have the tables turned on them at the final moment. While stealing another's formula was certainly unethical, the twists and turns of the affair had transformed the entire sequence into a source of amazement regarding the various factions' open and covert maneuvering. However, by the time Liu Qingdi finished his account, the assembled listeners realized that Ning Yi's role throughout the entire affair had been remarkably unremarkable.

A great talent might excel at poetry, but if he was mediocre in other areas, it actually lowered people's opinion of him. It suggested he was all talk and no substance, which naturally made him less interesting. Looking again at that young figure across the way, this assessment seemed reasonable—such a person might indeed possess impressive poetic talent due to natural gifts, but this would stem from his youth allowing him to "force new words to express sorrow,"* while his life experience remained ultimately insufficient. At that moment, they could see Ning Yi's figure in the distant teahouse gathering his belongings to settle his bill before disappearing from their line of sight.

"Ah, what a pity..."

Zhou Yong sighed, though whether he referred to missing the opportunity to meet Ning Yi or to the struggle between the Su and Wu families remained unclear. As he spoke, he glanced at his pair of somewhat dismissive children, his gaze carrying complex emotions while instructing his attendants to gather the group, then lowered his head in contemplation.

Only then did Liu Qingdi look toward the direction where Ning Yi had vanished, a flash of satisfaction and hatred flickering in his eyes.

Nearby, the pair of siblings exchanged a crafty glance, their lips curved in knowing smiles.

When the carriage driver lifted the curtain to inform him they had arrived home, Wu Qilong pushed aside the covering to emerge into the approaching evening light. The sun had begun its slanted descent, its rays no longer harsh as they poured golden illumination over the Wu family's grand estate, making the magnificent and imposing gates appear especially solemn. Whenever he saw this scene, he would involuntarily think of the family's... sense of dignity and honor.

He remembered asking his mother when he was small why their family's courtyard was particularly large, why their family's gate differed from others. His mother would say it was because the Wu family was Jiangning's foremost cloth merchant.

The Wu family was Jiangning's foremost cloth merchant...

In fact, particularly during recent times—this past month of constant rushing and bustling—this feeling had become extraordinarily clear in his heart, bringing back memories of the scenes when his parents and others around him had spoken those words from childhood, teaching him these fundamental truths.

Jiangning's foremost cloth merchant.

This represented a position achieved through how many people's efforts, and from childhood, what occupied his thoughts was how to transform this recognition into more than just being Jiangning's foremost cloth merchant. From an early age, he had confidently known he would inevitably achieve this goal, and even throughout the preceding period, he had once believed he could see certain success within his grasp.

The bright future he had once seen so clearly had gone dark this afternoon, leaving him so cold and numb he could barely remember the carriage ride home. His body felt weightless and unsteady, his limbs moving beyond his control as he tried to recall how he had even managed to descend from the teahouse and find his way here. As the thought that *all of this would cease to exist* echoed in his mind, he walked toward where his family would be gathered, with no idea how to begin telling them what had happened—only that it had to be said.

********************

When Wu Qihao returned home, the lamp-lighting had already begun, and the household atmosphere felt distinctly wrong. A servant informed him he was needed in the main hall, and while crossing the courtyard, he encountered Fifth Uncle,⁶ who hadn't emerged in ages, being supported by two maidservants as he made his way over. The Wu family's lamps burned with unusual abundance tonight.

He knew something major had finally occurred; while he had sensed this moment was inevitable as the Brilliant Gold Brocade continued to fade in the warehouse, the reality of it made his heart sink. With no time to exchange pleasantries with his slow-moving Fifth Uncle, he broke into a run toward the main hall.

His father, elder brother, Uncle Wu Chengjian, Third Uncle Wu Chengyuan, and even two of the family's most intimate cousins had already assembled—these two relatives held considerable shares and dividends within the family business, though none of the important decision-making stewards like Luo Minzhi were present. The hastily gathered family members had perhaps not yet eaten dinner, with simple meals placed beside each person, though none had any appetite to eat. Wu Qihao glanced around before walking forward.

He knew this was worse than the fading fabric—a crisis they should have been prepared for. His father's vacant eyes betrayed the dignified facade he unconsciously maintained while sitting apart from the others. It wasn't until Wu Qihao drew close that his father finally reacted, a cold, weary laugh escaping his lips.

"...The powerful in their red gates laugh at those preparing for office... Even white-haired friends still greet each other with a hand on the sword... heh..."⁵ That cold laughter carried no hostility, sounding merely weary. He looked at his second son before him and shook his head.

"Everyone was deceived by one person... he never took us seriously at all... oh, Qihao..."

"Father, what happened?"

"Have you eaten?"

"Have someone bring food—eat something first. There's been an incident; ask your elder brother about it..."

Wu Qihao watched his father close his eyes and rub his forehead, and when he reopened them, that gaze had stabilized, wiping away the moment's confusion to return to the restraint and ferocity befitting the head of the textile trade's leading family and Jiangning's foremost cloth merchant house. Yet after a moment, his eyes fixed on a corner of the room as he released a long, deep breath.

That was not a good sign.

Wu Qihao turned to walk toward his elder brother, who sat near the doorway in quiet contemplation, his gaze somewhat cold. At least his brother remained composed at this moment—he was thinking of countermeasures.

"Brother."

"Sit." Wu Qilong glanced at him and patted the space beside him. His period of panic had passed, and he could now contain his emotions. After his brother sat down, he spoke in calm tones: "The fabric in the warehouse continues fading. This afternoon while I was out, I encountered Ning Liheng, and then..." He paused as he noticed elderly Fifth Uncle entering through the doorway, rising with everyone else to show respect. "Then we had some conversation, and I finally understood the entire situation..."

The daylight had completely faded while the lamps grew brighter and the night deepened. Throughout the Wu family's grand estate, wind stirred the flickering flames as one after another person holding important positions within the Wu family began gathering toward the main hall.

These included family branch brothers like Wu Chenghou who shared control of the household and held authority to participate in important matters, clan relatives and connections who had genuine participation in the business side of this family, younger generation members like Wu Qilong and Wu Qihao who had received significant responsibilities and could now enter the inner circle of decision-making, or previous generation Wu family members who had once competed on equal terms with Su Yu in the marketplace. The Wu family—Jiangning's foremost cloth merchant—all these participants who were currently commanding wind and rain in the business world or had once done so had been alarmed by the current crisis and now required assembly for unified consultation and response.

Two months earlier, even during the struggle for imperial merchant status, the Wu family had never gathered even a quarter of these people, particularly Wu family patriarchs like Fifth Uncle and Eighth Uncle—once figures whose mere footsteps could make Jiangning's textile world tremble, now retired to peaceful old age, yet forced at this moment to emerge once more to confront this crisis.

Two months ago, the Wu family had pursued its objectives with ease, barely noticing the scholar who appeared to be acting out a frivolous comedy for their amusement. His actions, like the casual conversation and pouring of tea today, seemed utterly harmless. Yet in that time, the hand that poured the tea had, with that same casual ease, turned over to reveal a crushing killing intent, now thundering down upon them all.

(End of Chapter)

**Cultural Footnotes:**

¹ **Smiling tiger (笑面虎)**: A Chinese idiom for someone who appears friendly and harmless but is secretly malicious or dangerous.

² **Marriageable age (及笄之年)**: Traditional Chinese coming-of-age ceremony for girls at 15 years old, when they would receive their hairpin (ji) and be considered eligible for marriage. This marked the transition from childhood to womanhood in traditional Chinese society.

³ **Traditional Chinese architecture**: Flying eaves and interlocking bracket sets (斗拱) are ornate wooden brackets that support the curved rooflines characteristic of traditional Chinese buildings.

⁴ **Imperial family members (皇室闲人)**: "Idle royalty" - princes and other imperial family members who live in provincial cities with comfortable stipends but are deliberately kept away from the capital to prevent them from becoming political threats or succession rivals.

⁵ **Classical poetry reference**: Lines from Tang Dynasty poet Wang Wei: "The powerful in their red gates laugh at those preparing for office; Even white-haired friends still greet each other with a hand on the sword." This expresses cynical wisdom about political relationships - the established elite mock newcomers seeking advancement, while even lifelong friends remain wary and distrustful of each other in the treacherous world of power.

⁶ **Fifth Uncle (五叔公)**: Traditional Chinese family title meaning "Paternal Grand-uncle, fifth in seniority." These precise kinship terms immediately indicate the speaker's position within a large, hierarchical extended family structure where age and generational rank determine formal address and authority.
