When she woke again, it was already morning. The overcast sky threatened rain, and the air felt somewhat stifling. Her husband's figure no longer sat by the window. Xiao Chan had gone to rest, replaced by Juan'er and Xing'er keeping watch. According to Juan'er, Old Master had visited earlier. Seeing her asleep, he'd gestured not to wake her, only casually asking what those charts and diagrams on the table were for. Learning they were Ning Yi's work, he hadn't pressed further, simply instructing her to rest well.

A single night couldn't break the high fever. She drank another bowl of medicine, her head heavy and muddled, mouth full of bitterness. The anxiety still burned in her heart. Though Liheng had said those things yesterday, how would it all turn out in the end... She actually already knew, more or less. She just couldn't accept it, having put in so much effort.

That figure wasn't in the room. The thought left a vast emptiness in her heart, but drowsiness finally pulled her under again. This sleep wasn't like the pre-dawn rest—nightmares came in waves, leaving her no peace. When she woke again, it was probably past noon. Ning Yi sat once more in the chair by the window, speaking quietly with Juan'er standing beside him, apparently about some account details from last year. Juan'er explained the circumstances in hushed tones.

Xiao Chan approached. "Young Miss, you're awake?" Then Ning Yi and Juan'er turned as well.

Her body was exhausted. She didn't want to speak or move. Chan'er came to prop up her pillows, and Liheng's hand reached over to rest on her forehead—except for last night, they'd never had such intimate contact before, but it felt natural. After checking her temperature with his palm, the man nodded. "Seems a bit better. Have someone call Doctor Sun later." Then he muttered something about "probably around forty degrees yesterday" and other cryptic phrases.

Xiao Chan left to bring a bowl of porridge, "forcing" her to take a few sips of plain congee. Soon after, Doctor Sun arrived to check her condition. Xiao Chan occasionally spoke up while Liheng continued examining account books and making notes at the table, only commenting occasionally. She lay there watching everyone come and go. Sometimes she and Ning Yi were alone in the room together. The figure at the table moved with a swift, methodical precision that had a strangely calming effect, settling her spirit.

By evening, the sky had darkened and rain began falling outside the window, bringing fresh air with an earthy scent into the room.

She dozed on and off. That evening, Manager Liao and the others didn't enter the mansion. Through the rain, there were none of the restless lights that had flickered for days. What were they doing? Had the business situation changed? The thoughts drifted through her mind occasionally. Only Ning Yi, Chan'er, Juan'er, and Xing'er remained in the room with her. Several sheets of rice paper hung on the walls. Liheng would glance at them occasionally—a strange touch of meaning added to the quiet atmosphere.

Though her bedroom often handled business matters and appeared more stately than typical young ladies' chambers, it still had many feminine decorations that girls enjoyed. Now these papers disrupted that atmosphere entirely. The room remained mostly quiet. Chan'er, Juan'er, and Xing'er spoke softly with her and Liheng, coming and going gently. Mixed with the rain, Liheng's focused busyness followed its own rhythm, bringing only a quiet strength.

It wasn't until tonight that she more clearly recalled that pre-dawn thought: her husband was a scholar, even Jiangning's most accomplished talent.

In earlier years, before marriage, when she was still a girl, she'd dreamed of such things. She'd imagined more than once marrying some brilliantly talented scholar. Though she was a merchant's daughter, her family was a major merchant house—such opportunities weren't impossible.

After understanding the ways of the world better, such thoughts had lessened, though the dreams certainly remained. Those stories of talented scholars and beautiful ladies that spread through Jiangning, the later rising stars like Cao Guan and Li Pin, the tales of competitions at various poetry gatherings and banquets—she'd listened to them all with great interest. Even if she later attended Puyuan Poetry Gatherings mostly for business, hearing about other gatherings and seeing excellent poems still felt worthwhile. It was like something from another world, but that didn't stop her from liking and dreaming about it.

Then life remained life. She married according to plan, taking in a live-in husband. He was supposedly a scholar, but had nothing to do with talent—just a bookworm. Life was life. She could still dream about those great poets and talents, yet when her husband turned out less wooden than expected, when certain things began to overlap, she found herself at a loss.

Her husband was now called Jiangning's premier talent by some. What should she do? She couldn't quite grasp the symbols that should mark a great talent or scholar—from stories and operas, it was all clear. Great talents were supposed to speak in classical phrases, quote the classics, and be the center of attention wherever they went, maintaining an untouchable air. She'd once dreamed of marrying a great talent, expecting formal exchanges like "My lord has worked hard," "Thank you for your concern, my lady"—proper and distanced. But without that distance, how should things be?

Daily conversations were simple and casual. He was unpretentious, humble, witty and amusing. Yet this was the person others called the premier talent. She would look at those two poems from time to time. Between them, they weren't like ordinary husband and wife. Sometimes they were simply like friends—she'd never heard of such close friends. They would go upstairs every few days to chat about anything. Could men and women become such friends? Romance novels often featured women disguising as men to befriend others, but she hadn't disguised herself. Were there such married couples? She'd never heard of any.

She actually liked this feeling. She liked it so much she didn't know how to change it or take it further. As for recognizing her husband as a great talent, it had always been somewhat vague for her. Until now, when it became clear.

From afternoon to evening, she heard her husband quietly ask several questions, all touching on the most crucial points of account records from recent years. Her husband was intelligent—he was seriously doing these things. Su Tan'er understood this quickly, but even the most talented person couldn't help with this. Her husband was a great scholar. She was the merchant's daughter. These matters should have been hers to handle. She'd always worked hard to manage them well, striving not to let her husband feel troubled or disturbed by such affairs. But now, she'd finally dragged him into it.

How things would turn out didn't matter here. Her husband couldn't possibly handle this well. He was determined, saying "I'll definitely succeed." But such matters couldn't be resolved by determination alone. After bringing him in through marriage, she'd ultimately entangled him in these affairs...

She thought about these things, sleeping and waking intermittently. Around midnight, the rain continued, but the night felt quiet. Oil lamp light flickered. Only Liheng's silhouette sat in the chair. He was reading correspondence with various branch managers. Noticing movement behind him, he turned back, then set down the letter and rose to approach.

"Awake? Want some water?"

"Mm..." Su Tan'er nodded faintly.

Ning Yi raised her pillows and brought a cup of warm water, helping her sip a few mouthfuls. "Xing'er and Juan'er are asleep. Xiao Chan was exhausted today too, so I finally convinced her to get some rest. But you should probably be awake for your medicine soon. Um, if you need to..." Ning Yi looked at her hesitantly, then stood. "I'll call Xiao Chan."

Ning Yi smiled. "Feeling better?"

"Better..."

"The fever will probably take two or three more days to break." Ning Yi studied her for a moment. There was much that could be said, but finally he just shook his head. "Your heart's troubled. I won't bother you. Tell me if you want food or anything else."

He took a letter and sat in a chair not far away. The room fell quiet, the autumn rain outside long since becoming mere background. Su Tan'er watched that figure for a long time before finally speaking: "Husband... why did you agree to this marriage?"

They'd had similar conversations before, but asking now clearly meant something different. Ning Yi set down the letter, gazing at Su Tan'er on the bed. After a long moment, he smiled and shook his head. "I've thought about discussing this with you, but... perhaps in a few days, when you're more clear-headed? You don't look well now."

"I'm fine. I want... want to know." Su Tan'er spoke slowly. "Husband wanted to discuss this too?"

"It's not about why I agreed to this marriage." Ning Yi placed the letter aside and moved his chair to the bedside, sitting down. "I've already said—I don't actually know why I agreed to this marriage. I lost those memories. What I want to discuss isn't what happened before the memory loss, but... what came after."

"What came after?"

Ning Yi looked at her for a while, sighed, then smiled. "Are you sure you want to hear this now?"

Su Tan'er struggled to smile too. "If I don't listen, I won't be able to sleep..."

"Alright." Ning Yi nodded. Since Su Tan'er's mental agility was probably limited right now, he spoke slowly, occasionally repeating himself. "It's actually quite simple. We'll definitely discuss it someday... A man named Ning Yi married a woman named Su Tan'er. It was a live-in arrangement, so that's how we came to know each other... These are already facts. Let's not dwell on them. Call it fate or coincidence—either way, it's the two of us now... This is how things are. You're part of it. How do you see it?"

Su Tan'er frowned in thought, not quite grasping Ning Yi's meaning. "I... I'm very happy..."

Ning Yi patted her hand, pausing slightly. "What others say doesn't matter—talented scholar, live-in husband... The fact is, things are already like this. Life is simple. Speaking for myself, I have no issues with being a live-in husband. As for you, I don't dislike you... No, let me be direct—I like you. Your business sense, your personality. You're strong-willed but good. I like that personality. Besides, you're quite pretty too..."

Ning Yi rested his chin on one hand at the bedside, his words calm and even, as if casually saying whatever came to mind. But Su Tan'er was suddenly caught off guard. Even in her current weakened state, a blush spread across her face. She stammered shyly: "Husband, husband... do you really... like me?"

"Mm, I do like you."

"But... but this isn't how a proper young lady is supposed to be... 'A woman's virtue is her lack of talent.' Women shouldn't... shouldn't be like me. Everyone says... that men don't like women like this..." She spoke disjointedly for a while, her fevered state not preventing her from emphasizing certain points with her eyes. After a moment, she grew dejected. "I... I'm not good enough for you..."

"Still arguing even now? In others' eyes, I'm just a freeloader. You're more capable than many men..."

"Husband isn't incompetent..."

Ning Yi smiled. "No need to keep putting ourselves down. These things don't matter. Arguments about them could last until tomorrow with no result... The point is, it's just between us. I'm satisfied with life. I like you, this courtyard, Chan'er, Juan'er, Xing'er, and the rest. Some boring people around doing boring things, but overall, continuing like this is fine. It's quite good. So I wanted to tell you this."

He took Su Tan'er's weak hand in his. Her fingers were slender and rounded, beautiful. He gently turned them over in his palm before letting them rest. "If you're not too dissatisfied either, then we'll probably continue like this... Regardless of past arrangements, things are already this way. No need to consider why anymore. As long as we don't dislike each other, that's enough..."

He squeezed Su Tan'er's hand, waiting for an answer. For Ning Yi, this was quite an important decision too. When he'd first awakened, he'd been prepared to leave at any time, later simply observing how things developed. But now, some matters could probably be settled. He didn't need to overthink it. They were already husband and wife, he liked her character, and they got along well. Whether they changed things or not didn't matter anymore. What came next was simply life.

Su Tan'er looked at him, blinking once, twice. Her eyes reddened and tears fell. She pressed her lips tightly together, unable to speak. Ning Yi waited a long while before lowering his head with a smile. "No matter what, you should give me an answer..."

"Husband..." Su Tan'er's lips moved. "After this matter passes, when I'm better, we..." Her voice was choked and slightly hoarse, yet her gaze held determination. In the rainy night, she paused briefly, sniffling.

"Let's consummate our marriage..."

Getting her to say those words really wasn't easy...

(End of Chapter)

¹ *Rice paper: Traditional Chinese paper made from rice straw, commonly used for calligraphy and painting*

² *Classical particles (之乎者也): Function words in classical Chinese that became symbols of pedantic scholarly speech*

³ *A woman's virtue lies in her lack of talent (女子无才便是德): Traditional saying reflecting historical gender expectations*

⁴ *Consummate marriage (圆房): Traditional term for the physical consummation that completes a marriage ceremony*
