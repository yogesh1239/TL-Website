In the early afternoon, the market streets along the Qinhuai River were bustling with people. With the spring thaw, merchants and travelers were once again crisscrossing through Jiangning. Long-distance travelers could be seen leading horses through the marketplace, while nearby escort agencies and merchant caravans prepared their gear for departure or paused to relax after arriving from neighboring cities, their armed escorts gazing around and loudly discussing the city's prosperity with their companions.

Once they left the city and truly set out on their journeys, such prosperous scenes would become rare.

Shops lining both sides of the road hung fluttering banners or signboards in front of their doors, with those near the river having stone steps and landings for boarding boats or washing clothes. Though the snow had only recently melted and the surrounding willows had yet to sprout, birds were already singing melodiously overhead while painted pleasure boats glided across the green waves, their music and singing drifting through the air.

"Teacher, look—that scholar on the painted boat seems to be named Yuan Li. He also visited Imperial Son-in-law Grandfather a few days ago."

In this early spring weather, it was hard to feel too tense about anything. At this moment, Ning Yi was carrying a long bundle on his back, walking along the street with the little commandery princess Zhou Pei. Behind them to the side was a middle-aged man dressed in low-key clothing but with quite a sturdy build. This man was a mansion guard surnamed Song, named Qian. When Zhou Pei and Junwu attended classes at Yushan Academy, two guards always waited nearby. Now that Zhou Pei wanted to go with Ning Yi to find Kang Xian, when she carried several fire lances out of that small courtyard, she naturally called one of them to follow along.

This man had served as a guard at the prince's mansion for a long time. As long as nothing major happened, he was like an invisible person, giving off little sense of his presence.

The academy and the Imperial Son-in-law's Mansion were quite far apart, but since they had nothing urgent to do anyway, Ning Yi preferred to stroll through the city for a while. Little Zhou Pei had something on her mind, but naturally couldn't speak it aloud. As she walked with Ning Yi for a while and listened to this teacher point things out and discuss various learned and interesting topics, she temporarily set aside her mood. Walking along the riverside road now, she saw a blue-robed young gentleman standing at the bow of a painted boat not far away. Recalling what she knew about him, she brought it up with Ning Yi.

Ning Yi turned his head to look that direction. The painted boat was quite lively—clearly another gathering. The blue-robed gentleman stood at the bow, a folding fan in hand and a silk headband fluttering above, cutting quite a refined figure. A white-clothed young woman also emerged from the painted boat and stood beside him to talk—most likely one of the accompanying courtesans from the boat. Her figure was quite good, though from this distance her features couldn't be made out clearly.

Turning back to look at Zhou Pei, he saw her holding up her long skirt slightly with one hand so she wouldn't dirty the hem while walking, while craning her neck to gaze at the painted boat with great interest. At this moment, she looked like a gossiping little fangirl chasing celebrities—quite adorable.

"...It seems to be Bright Jade Pavilion's boat. I wonder if that's Yin Xue or Hua Ping. Teacher, guess what they're saying?"

That Bright Jade Pavilion had some fame in Jiangning, and the two young women Yin Xue and Hua Ping were its star attractions. This sort of thing wasn't considered bad nowadays—as long as there were talented scholars and beautiful women, romanticize it a bit and it became a celebrated story. Zhou Pei had grown up hearing such tales, so she was quite interested now. Ning Yi squinted his eyes and looked as well. The beautiful painted boat sailing through the early spring atmosphere was indeed pleasing to the eye.

"That name Yuan Li sounds familiar. Is he very impressive?"

Zhou Pei had wanted to nod and say yes, but then her mouth pressed together, and she blinked at her teacher instead. That person did have some reputation—being able to visit Kang Xian together with others and actually have things to discuss proved it. But in Jiangning now, if put alongside those five words "Ning Yi, courtesy name Liheng," it seemed somewhat inadequate. Looking at this teacher carrying his long bundle on his back, smiling as he asked without seeming to fake it, Zhou Pei felt somewhat helpless for a moment. Her interest in the talented scholar and beautiful woman over there diminished somewhat, and she just muttered a response.

"He's alright. A few days ago at Imperial Son-in-law Grandfather's place, when discussing northern affairs, he was quite passionate. Didn't teacher also sign your name on that memorial? He was one of the most enthusiastic about it. These days I hear he's been discussing these things with people. Today must be the same... It's a shame that the Jin Kingdom and Liao Kingdom made peace. Who knows how many years before war breaks out again. Otherwise, I heard he wanted to follow Ban Chao's example and abandon his writing brush to join the military..."¹

"It won't take many years actually..."

Ning Yi smiled and nodded. He, Zhou Pei, and Song Qian continued walking forward, while the painted boat also slowly moved along. Inside came music and dancing, and scholars could faintly be heard reciting poetry, mixing the Qinhuai River's leisurely atmosphere with the marketplace's bustling noise. Zhou Pei took small quick steps at his side: "Teacher also says it won't take many years? A few days ago when we went with Imperial Son-in-law Grandfather to visit Grandfather Qin's place, they guessed the same thing..."

The little girl frowned, then as if remembering something, said mysteriously: "Teacher, do you know about Grandfather Qin's affairs?"

"What affairs?"

"Um... that matter about the Black Water Alliance. In past years I only knew that Grandfather Qin's scholarship was very impressive and that Imperial Son-in-law Grandfather had a good friendship with him, but I didn't know what he had done. Only recently did I suddenly start hearing people talk about it." Zhou Pei thought for a moment. "Something about the Black Water Alliance, about losing battles... For years, Grandfather Qin lived here in obscurity with few visitors, but recently more people have been visiting and talking about him. Privately, though, I've heard some curse him for fishing for fame, with some even calling him a traitor to the Han people..."

The little girl frowned: "Recently I asked Imperial Son-in-law Grandfather, but he won't say much, only that all matters should wait until the coffin is sealed before conclusions can be drawn,² and now isn't the time to speak of it. I roughly know it might have something to do with the Jin Kingdom and Liao Kingdom, but every time Imperial Son-in-law Grandfather visits Grandfather Qin, Grandfather Qin refuses to discuss these things, only chatting about this and that, as if he doesn't care about these matters at all..."

"That was years ago. I'm not really clear on it either..." Ning Yi thought for a moment, then shook his head. Recently he'd roughly heard some of the rumors spreading through the streets—only these rumors were told in fantastic and exaggerated ways, not reliable. He only knew that the rumors concerned the Black Water Alliance from seven years ago, where Old Qin had supposedly signed a chaotic and humiliating treaty. People now bringing this up claimed the seeds of the Jin-Liao conflict were planted back then and thus dug out Qin Siyuan's name, though most naturally didn't believe it.

In the past, Old Qin and Kang Xian both loved discussing the world's situation, but recently they'd talked very little about northern affairs—this was true. Especially recently when the Jin Kingdom and Liao Kingdom suddenly made peace and Yelu Yanxi conferred the title of Great Sacred Emperor on Wanyan Aguda—when news of this arrived, it was like pouring a huge basin of cold water on the Wu Dynasty people who had been anticipating war between Jin and Liao. In theory, whether real or fake, this step backward by the Liao emperor Yelu Yanxi would at least preserve several years of peace. The war that seemed about to start would be delayed again. At such a time, when Ning Yi occasionally visited Old Qin, he discovered the old man wouldn't even discuss this matter anymore, seemingly paying it no mind at all. On the other hand, Kang Xian's visits to Old Qin's place had vaguely increased.

Ning Yi could sense certain atmospheres and naturally had his guesses. But he wasn't a true participant. Even Kang Xian, who knew the real inside story, was holding his breath and not opening his mouth about this at such a time. In such serious matters, he certainly shouldn't make confident pronouncements and talk nonsense to Zhou Pei. He just felt that people who did great things were fundamentally different from those who only talked about them. Whether selling out the country or misleading it, the feeling was still different from discussing abandoning scholarship for military service on painted boats amidst rouge and powder.

He spoke with Zhou Pei about matters concerning the Jin Kingdom and Liao Kingdom. That Wanyan Aguda had single-handedly revived the Jurchen people in the white mountains and black waters, creating the myth that "Jurchens are invincible when numbering less than ten thousand, and unstoppable when reaching ten thousand."³ At Hubudagang he'd even achieved the outrageous feat of defeating seven hundred thousand with twenty thousand—truly an extraordinary hero to the extreme. Not many in the Wu Dynasty truly understood this information, but over the past year, through Ning Yi's casual storytelling, a group of students including Zhou Pei both admired and feared this man. But fortunately, the Jurchen population was small, and Wanyan Aguda wasn't young anymore. The possibility of him destroying Liao and then threatening the Wu Dynasty in his lifetime still wasn't very high.

Zhou Pei enjoyed discussing these matters. After speculating for a while, she'd ask: "Right? Right?" After talking about these things for a bit, the depression she'd felt earlier temporarily lifted. At noon she'd only nibbled on a small meat wrap because of her gloomy mood. Having now walked quite a distance, her stomach was hungry. Just then they approached the newly opened Zhu's restaurant. The little girl hinted around, requesting they stop to rest a bit and eat something, while also taking a look at this new Zhu's establishment—in the past, Ning Yi had taken her and her brother to Zhu's main location to eat a few times.

The two headed that direction. Just as they approached the shop entrance, they encountered two people coming from that direction. One of these two looked about fifty years old, tall and thin. Though dressed as a scholar, he had an air of wealth and noble bearing cultivated from years of commanding others. His expression was stern, his gaze proud. Ning Yi had met this man once before—he was a famous great scholar in Jiangning named Zhang Rui, courtesy name Hongyuan. He was also one of Prince Kang's Mansion's tutors and was said to be quite valued.

The other man looked about thirty, slightly plump, also squinting with a stern expression and dressed as a scholar, holding a fan. Ning Yi didn't recognize this man. Zhang Rui recognized Ning Yi, and after exchanging a few words with his companion, the two of them came over. But first Zhou Pei went forward and bowed: "Teacher Zhang, Teacher Li, greetings."

The two men quickly returned the courtesy: "The commandery princess is here as well. We don't deserve such honor, we don't deserve it."

Then they greeted Ning Yi. After introductions were made, it turned out both these men were tutors at Prince Kang's Mansion and also had considerable talent and fame in Jiangning. That plump man surnamed Li was named Li Tong. Squinting as he sized up Ning Yi: "So you are Ning Yi, courtesy name Liheng. I've long admired your great name but never had the chance to meet you. Today this is truly a fortunate encounter."

Zhang Rui looked at Ning Yi and Zhou Pei with some displeasure: "Liheng actually brought the commandery princess's precious body to wander through such marketplace streets. This seems somewhat... not quite proper, does it?"

If this were an ordinary chance meeting, they'd probably exchange pleasantries for a bit and then part ways. But after speaking a few sentences, the two Zhang and Li began bringing up topics like "humbly requesting guidance"⁴ and "sitting down to discuss the Way together," and talking about how Ning Yi's teaching methods and bringing the little commandery princess to walk through marketplace streets like this was really quite improper.

These matters did have their reasons. At the end of last year when little Pei and Junwu formally acknowledged Ning Yi as their teacher, the original plan had been to make it a grand affair, with Prince Kang personally visiting the Su family to add momentum, but Ning Yi had refused, so the acknowledgment ceremony was simplified with Kang Xian serving as intermediary. However, as tutors at Prince Kang's Mansion, these people knew that the young prince and young princess had gained another teacher.

The prince's mansion had many retainers, and a group of tutors had considerable fame and status and weren't badly off, but they mainly taught the children of various servants in the mansion. Even if some had teacher-student relationships with the young prince and princess, it was still different from the kind of teacher that a family would specifically and deliberately seek out.

Ning Yi was just over twenty years old and called Jiangning's Number One Talent—it was impossible for him not to be resented. Resentful, the tutors had organized a poetry gathering at a brothel and sent Ning Yi an invitation, assuming their collective status at the prince's mansion made the summons impossible to refuse. They didn't realize Ning Yi wasn't truly a colleague—he didn't work at the mansion and only had a guest badge that Kang Xian had pushed on him. Besides, with his and Su Tan'er's marital relationship at a new peak of passion after finally consummating their two-year marriage, he had simply tossed the invitation aside and sent back a mass-produced rejection letter—eloquently written at great length, but the meaning was just seven words: Busy, not going, sorry.

These people had spent half a month specifically reviewing books and preparing questions and such, but when their expectations fell through, they were quite indignant. Today when Zhang and Li ran into Ning Yi on the street, that Li fellow had originally been somewhat apprehensive about "Jiangning's Number One Talent," but seeing Ning Yi looking wet behind the ears—perhaps having some talent and worthy of the word "unusual," but definitely not "broadly learned" or "profoundly skilled"—right then he and Zhang Rui decided to compete with this person in scholarship today, though of course they called it "humbly requesting guidance."

You might be able to refuse "requesting guidance," but when people say they just want to sit down and chat for a bit, there's no way to refuse. Soon after, the group headed toward the nearby newly opened Zhu's restaurant, then went upstairs and found a private room to sit down.

FOOTNOTES:

¹ *Ban Chao (32-102 AD) was a famous Eastern Han Dynasty official who "abandoned his writing brush to join the military" (投笔从戎), leaving scholarly pursuits to become a legendary military commander on the northwestern frontier. This became a classical expression for intellectuals choosing military service over scholarly careers.*

² *Classical Chinese idiom meaning "only when the coffin is sealed can conclusions be drawn"—one can fairly judge a person's life only after it's complete.*

³ *Famous saying about Jurchen military prowess: "When Jurchens number fewer than ten thousand, they cannot be matched; when they number ten thousand, they cannot be opposed." Wanyan Aguda's victory at the Battle of Hubudagang (20,000 defeating 700,000) made this legend reality.*

⁴ *In Chinese scholar culture, direct intellectual challenges would cause both parties to lose face. Competition must be politely framed as "requesting teaching" (讨教) or "scholarly discussion," maintaining courteous surface while everyone understands the competitive intent—a social politeness trap.*
