"...Uh... 'Settling Wind Waves'?"

On the small lawn outside the outer courtyard of Yushan Academy, Su Chonghua was smiling as he glanced at the paper, his brow furrowing slightly as he recognized the poem's significance just as Little Seven ran over, calling out cheerfully, "Uncle Mountain Chief." He responded with an absent "Mm," his attention already fixed on the manuscript, which drew his scholarly eye despite his initial casual interest.

"Uncle Mountain Chief, that's mine," Little Seven said with polite formality, bowing respectfully before looking hopefully at the manuscript paper. Her expression carried that particular mixture of reverence and possessiveness that only children could manage when protecting something precious, and Su Chonghua found himself charmed by her earnestness even as his academic curiosity deepened.

He handed the draft back to her, watching with growing intrigue as she carefully folded it with the reverent attention of someone handling a sacred text, preparing to tuck it safely into her robes. The hesitation in his smile stemmed not from any reluctance to return the paper, but from the implications of what he'd glimpsed in those few opening lines—he recognized the handwriting all too well, and the choice of poem title sent currents of scholarly speculation through his mind.

"Settling Wind Waves"¹... The weight of that particular title carried meanings that made him reconsider the casual dismissal he'd been cultivating toward his academy's resident son-in-law.

"Little Seven," he said gently, his curiosity overcoming propriety, "could you let Uncle take another look at that?"

"Ah?" She paused mid-motion, blinking those wide eyes before understanding dawned, and with a soft "Oh," she dutifully handed the manuscript back with both hands. Her lips pressed together as she watched him, clearly wanting to remind the Mountain Chief not to damage the precious paper but recognizing that such instructions would be terribly impolite—her internal struggle between protectiveness and proper manners played across her young features with transparent honesty.

Su Chonghua accepted the manuscript with appropriate solemnity, unfolding it carefully while noting how Little Seven's expression shifted between worry and trust. As he read through the complete work once, then again more slowly, his scholarly mind engaged fully with the layers of meaning embedded in the verses, and his frown deepened not from displeasure but from the uncomfortable recognition that his previous assessment of Ning Yi's capabilities might have been colored by personal bias rather than objective analysis.

The silence stretched as he processed both the technical excellence of the composition and its deeper implications, finally releasing a complex sigh that carried equal parts admiration and frustration before looking back at Little Seven with a renewed smile that held traces of his internal revelation.

"Little Seven, this poem..." he began, then paused as he considered how to frame his thoughts appropriately for a child's understanding.

"It's mine," she said with simple certainty, her tone carrying the absolute conviction that only comes from complete trust.

"Indeed, I know it's yours," he replied with genuine warmth, though his scholarly mind continued analyzing the sophisticated craftsmanship behind the verses. "Did Master Liheng write this especially for you?"

"Yes," Little Seven nodded earnestly, then corrected herself with careful precision that suggested she'd been coached on the importance of accuracy: "Yi... er, the Master traded it with Little Seven, saying it belonged to me now and that I should take good care of it."

"I see." Su Chonghua nodded thoughtfully, processing the implications of Ning Yi treating such a sophisticated work as a casual gift to a child, before carefully returning the manuscript to her small hands. "You must indeed take very good care of it."

After Little Seven departed with her treasured poem safely secured, Su Chonghua remained standing in the courtyard, watching her small figure disappear into the academy's bustling life while his mind worked through the uncomfortable reassessment that the morning's encounter had forced upon him. Liheng clearly possessed genuine literary talent, a recognition that came grudgingly but undeniably given the evidence he'd just examined, yet the man's apparent indifference to proper literary circles suggested either admirable humility or problematic isolation from serious intellectual discourse.

The choice to write "Settling Wind Waves" and present it as a casual gift to a child carried multiple interpretations: perhaps it represented the self-aware acknowledgment of someone who understood that releasing such work publicly might invite ridicule for its current circumstances, or perhaps it demonstrated the kind of casual mastery that comes from genuine confidence rather than desperate seeking of approval.

Ning Yi had become irrelevant to his concerns anyway—the afternoon held its own responsibilities, evening social obligations awaited, and a poetry gathering demanded his attention. Pushing the morning's revelations aside with the practiced efficiency of someone accustomed to compartmentalizing scholarly pursuits from social necessities, he turned his attention to more immediate academic matters that required his oversight.

The afternoon's apparent tranquility throughout Jiangning's streets concealed the underlying tensions that gripped the textile industry's leadership, though ordinary citizens remained blissfully unaware of the commercial warfare being waged in their midst. Pedestrians moved through bustling lanes while tea houses filled with the gentle sounds of entertainment, and occasional conversations touched on local gossip that sometimes mentioned the Su and Wu family businesses without grasping the full scope of their ongoing conflict.

During this deceptively peaceful afternoon, in a deliberately chosen tea house that offered both privacy and the appearance of casual commerce, Su Tan'er arrived for what would prove to be a pivotal confrontation, accompanied by carefully selected managers whose loyalty had been thoroughly tested along with her three trusted maids. The location itself had been chosen strategically—quiet enough for sensitive negotiations yet public enough to prevent any accusations of improper secret dealings.

Wu Chenghou led the Wu family delegation, his opening words cutting straight through any pretense of social pleasantries. "Why didn't Ning Yi come?" he demanded, his tone making it clear that her husband's absence rankled him far more than her presence. He had clearly hoped to confront the man he perceived as the true power behind the Su family's recent maneuvers, a fact Su Tan'er's response was crafted to exploit.

"My husband has never cared for such marketplace scheming," she replied with perfect composure, her tone suggesting that such matters were beneath his elevated concerns. "After writing that poem of advice to Uncle that day, he hasn't asked about it again. I dare not trouble him further with such matters."

Though Su Tan'er typically commanded respect through her demonstrated competence and unwavering strength in business negotiations, this deliberate adoption of the helpless wife persona served to infuriate Wu Chenghou precisely because it reminded him of how thoroughly he'd been outmaneuvered while simultaneously suggesting that the entire crisis represented nothing more than casual amusement for her husband. The reference to "that poem of advice to Uncle" particularly stung because it forced him to remember "Those who succeed first mock the ambitious"—that pointed reminder of his family's failure to grasp the changing dynamics until far too late, delivered with the casual elegance of someone offering friendly guidance to an intellectual inferior.

"So you're saying that your esteemed husband truly doesn't consider what you and so many of us from the Wu family are doing worth even a glance," Wu Chenghou replied with bitter sarcasm, his attempt to regain dignity through formal language only emphasizing how completely the situation had slipped beyond his control.

Su Tan'er's smile carried the serene confidence of someone holding all the important cards as she continued her performance of devoted wife concealing superior strategy: "Uncle misunderstands. It's just that I'm too stubborn by nature, taking success and failure too seriously. If not for pitying Tan'er's illness that day, my husband wouldn't have intervened in such a trivial matter." 

Her explanation built systematically toward a crushing revelation. "As for today's events, my husband foresaw them two months ago," she said. "Every subsequent development, every single detail, has been arranged clearly. I'll explain it all to Uncle shortly. My husband's talent and strategic vision far exceed mine, Uncle needn't be angry about this..." His only response was a sharp, frustrated grunt, expressing a complete inability to formulate any adequate reply to this systematic demolition of his position, delivered with a gentle reasonableness that made opposition seem petty.

The wind that swept past the tea house carried their tense exchange into the afternoon air while ordinary life continued around them, pedestrians moving through nearby streets with no awareness of the commercial warfare being concluded in their midst. As the afternoon progressed through these charged negotiations, Su Tan'er eventually returned to her carriage with her three maids, followed by the managers whose discretion had made this entire operation possible.

The vehicles carried thick ledgers and account books that represented the Wu family's forced capitulation, and as Su Tan'er drew back the carriage curtain to observe the evening sky painted in shades of approaching dusk, her laughter bubbled up with the pure joy of complete victory achieved through careful planning and flawless execution.

"Chan'er," she asked suddenly, startling her maid with the unexpected question, "do you think what I said to those Wu family people earlier sounded domineering?"

"Eh?" Xiao Chan blinked in confusion, clearly unprepared for such direct solicitation of her opinion on her mistress's negotiating style. "What? Um, domineering... yes..."

"Mm, I thought I was quite impressive too," Su Tan'er continued with obvious satisfaction, nodding approvingly at her own performance while her maids exchanged glances that suggested their young mistress was displaying an unusually playful mood despite maintaining her essential character.

Throughout the negotiations, she'd maintained her typical composed professionalism while wielding her underlying strength with surgical precision, though her opening remarks about her husband's involvement had been so extravagantly flattering that all three maids had felt their cheeks burning with second-hand embarrassment. The Wu family representatives had eventually stopped mentioning Ning Yi entirely, suspecting that Su Tan'er was deliberately mocking them with exaggerated praise for her supposedly brilliant spouse.

"But Miss, after you told the Wu family all those things about Master, Manager Cao and the others looked completely shocked," Xiao Chan continued with barely suppressed giggles. "They still don't understand why the Wu family secretly handed over so much to us, and during the break, I heard them discussing: 'Oh, so Master Ning is really that capable?' These past two months have been the happiest time."

The managers and administrators following in the rear carriages weren't among the Su family's most influential figures, but they represented the carefully vetted middle-tier personnel that Ning Yi and Su Tan'er had jointly identified as trustworthy and uninvolved in the family's internal conflicts. Even if the Wu family had cooperated fully in transferring their entire operation to Su family control, the resulting administrative work would prove enormously complex, and while Xiao Chan, Juan'er, and Xing'er could provide valuable assistance, they certainly couldn't handle such intricate business details alone—the coming days would require these managers' expertise and discretion.

From the moment Su Tan'er had summoned them to participate while demanding absolute secrecy, she hadn't explained the full scope of their mission, leaving Manager Cao and his colleagues uncertain about the underlying reasons for these unusual arrangements. However, having worked within the Su family during the past two and a half months of mounting crisis, they understood the general situation perfectly well, and witnessing this sudden dramatic reversal while listening to Su Tan'er's exchanges with the Wu family representatives and observing their opponents' expressions of frustrated rage mixed with helpless acceptance, they immediately grasped that the Wu family had suffered a devastating defeat that transformed apparent Su family collapse into total victory.

Such a massive turnaround, maintained with such absolute secrecy and apparently orchestrated primarily by Master Ning, forced these experienced business professionals to completely revise their understanding of the young man they'd previously dismissed as an ineffective academic whose presence in the household represented more burden than benefit. During these past months, they'd watched the main branch's situation deteriorate while privately criticizing Ning Yi's apparent uselessness and feeling sympathy for Su Tan'er's unfortunate circumstances, occasionally sharing pessimistic assessments during casual conversations.

Now, during brief breaks in their intensive work, they found themselves marveling at the sophisticated planning that this apparently idle couple had conducted in complete secrecy, managing complex operations that had completely escaped their notice despite their involvement in the family's daily business affairs. According to Su Tan'er's confident assertions about absorbing whatever they could manage from the Wu family's resources, their opponents would likely never recover from this systematic dismantling of their commercial foundation.

At this moment, only the handful of people in those carriages understood the true scope of the day's triumphant developments. As they traveled homeward, they encountered Ning Yi returning from his own afternoon activities, and Su Tan'er stopped her carriage to invite him aboard while the managers in the following vehicles regarded him with transformed expressions that mixed admiration, bewilderment, speculation, and newfound respect in complex combinations that suggested their entire understanding of their employer's husband had undergone fundamental revision.

After Su Tan'er briefly conferred with the managers about the next day's arrangements, the groups separated temporarily, with the business teams departing to begin implementing their expanded responsibilities while the family carriage continued toward the Su residence.

Only upon reaching home did Su Tan'er discover that her cousin had arrived that day for an extended visit. Su Danhong's presence represented both welcome family connection and potential complication, since Ning Yi had met her previously and understood the complex dynamics between the cousins well enough to recognize that his own role in recent events might not receive entirely sympathetic evaluation from someone who prioritized traditional family stability over commercial innovation.

The subsequent family reunion proved less harmonious than Su Tan'er had hoped, with Su Danhong maintaining a coolly polite distance from Ning Yi that clearly communicated her disapproval of his influence on her cousin's life, though she avoided direct confrontation out of respect for social propriety. Understanding the source of this tension and having no desire to worsen family relationships during what should be a celebratory time, Ning Yi excused himself after dinner to take a solitary walk around the neighborhood, though he'd barely reached the courtyard gate when Su Tan'er caught up to offer an apology for her cousin's behavior.

"It's nothing," he reassured her with genuine understanding, recognizing Su Danhong's concerns as natural protectiveness rather than personal hostility. "She's worried about you and feeling pressure too. I won't take it to heart. Go back and keep her company."

"Mm." Su Tan'er bit her lip, then said, "Let Xiao Chan accompany you," calling her maid over to accompany Ning Yi on his evening stroll while she returned to manage the delicate family dynamics that required her immediate attention.

As mutual confidantes who understood each other's perspectives through years of close observation, Su Danhong recognized that her emotional responses were creating difficulties for Su Tan'er regardless of their underlying justification, and she offered a qualified apology that still maintained her essential position: "I'm sorry, but... if it weren't for him, you wouldn't have lost the imperial merchant contract after preparing for so many years—I can't help feeling sorry about that waste of your efforts..."

"Sister Hong, you don't understand the full situation..." Su Tan'er began, but her cousin's next question cut straight to more personal concerns that had been troubling her throughout her visit.

"You two still haven't consummated your marriage, have you?" Su Danhong's observation came from examining her cousin's bedroom arrangements, which clearly indicated continued separate sleeping quarters despite their long marriage. "Actually... consider this a joke if you want, but if you'd married Manager Xi back then, things might have turned out differently."

"Sister Hong, please don't make jokes like that anymore," Su Tan'er replied with a frown that conveyed genuine displeasure rather than mere social correction, her tone carrying a finality that suggested this topic had moved beyond casual teasing into genuinely offensive territory.

"Mm?" Su Danhong's eyebrows rose in genuine puzzlement at the strength of her cousin's reaction, prompting her to probe more deeply into what she clearly perceived as an incomprehensible situation: "What exactly are your feelings toward this husband of yours?"

"I... I don't really know myself..." Su Tan'er admitted with a shy headshake, her cheeks flushing slightly as she contemplated emotions that remained frustratingly difficult to categorize or explain even to herself.

The question of her feelings toward Ning Yi had indeed become increasingly complex and impossible to reduce to simple categories. As Su Danhong had observed, their marriage had lasted long enough that the continued lack of physical consummation suggested an unusual degree of emotional distance, and if Su Tan'er's husband had remained the bookish scholar she'd originally expected, she probably would have accepted her duty and completed the marriage's physical aspects out of resigned accommodation rather than genuine desire.

But their actual relationship had evolved far beyond such simple expectations, creating what people a thousand years later might recognize as a courtship process, though no woman of this era typically enjoyed such extended opportunities for genuine mutual discovery. Finding herself in uncharted emotional territory that combined curiosity, anticipation, and genuine affection in proportions she couldn't quite measure, Su Tan'er had reached the point where completing their marriage represented something far more significant than mere duty fulfillment.

The opportunity to speak openly about consummation had first arisen during her recent illness, but subsequent recovery had been followed immediately by the intense demands of managing the Wu family situation, requiring her complete attention to stabilize their current victory before she could properly arrange the personal celebration that this achievement deserved. By now, the matter had gained such importance in her mind that she refused to approach it casually or carelessly, wanting their union to carry proper symbolic weight while simultaneously avoiding any public awareness of how long they'd delayed, which might prompt gossip about her husband's adequacy or interest.

The timing considerations alone created substantial complexity—she needed meaningful ceremony without external observation, romantic significance without family interference, and perfect privacy that wouldn't generate speculation about why their relationship had required such extended development.

That night, sharing sleeping quarters with her cousin as family tradition dictated, Su Tan'er tugged gently at Su Danhong's sleeve before whispering with barely audible voice: "Sister Hong, when husband and wife live together... how exactly do they... manage things..."

Even someone as typically confident and assertive as Su Tan'er found herself reduced to mosquito-like whispers when discussing such intimate matters, and Su Danhong's current emotional state made her cousin's question sound particularly melancholy, as if she were preparing for reluctant surrender rather than joyful union.

"Why are you asking about this now?" Su Danhong responded with her own conflicted feelings clearly evident in her voice.

"Well... when we got married... I ran away before hearing... hearing Mother and the aunties explain these things..." Su Tan'er admitted with embarrassed honesty. Her wedding-day escape had left her without the traditional guidance she couldn't possibly seek from her maids, leaving her awkwardly dependent on the very family counsel she'd previously avoided.

Su Danhong felt a surge of sympathy mixed with frustration as she contemplated her cousin's situation, remembering conversations with her father and Xi Junyu about the family's commercial difficulties and Su Tan'er's limited options. Her strong-willed cousin was probably attempting to shed her businesswoman identity and accept traditional wifely submission as the most realistic adaptation to circumstances beyond her control, but given her husband's role in creating those circumstances, such resigned acceptance seemed unlikely to produce genuine happiness in their future domestic life.

This perspective colored Su Danhong's interactions with Ning Yi throughout the following days, maintaining the cool politeness that conveyed disapproval without descending into outright hostility, though Ning Yi responded with equal emotional distance that prevented either party from claiming moral superiority in their unstated conflict. Whenever Su Danhong observed Ning Yi and Su Tan'er together, she experienced the painful sensation of watching magnificent flowers being inserted into manure piles, or precious vegetables being devoured by pigs—metaphorically transforming Ning Yi into a swine enthusiastically rooting through the cabbage that represented her beloved cousin's wasted potential.

Naturally, Su Danhong remained completely unaware that her "cabbage" cousin was currently contemplating how to tie red silk ribbons around herself to make the anticipated "rooting process" more ceremonially meaningful and romantically satisfying, rather than simply enduring it with passive resignation.

Only by living within the Su family household could Su Danhong fully appreciate the mounting pressure bearing down on the main branch as the clan assembly deadline approached with relentless inevitability. The second and third branches conducted their preparations with obvious satisfaction, celebrating their assured victory while the main branch found itself increasingly isolated from family support, surrounded by relatives whose expressions seemed to constantly remind them that their loss of influence was both imminent and irreversible.

Even Su Danhong had to acknowledge the apparent hopelessness of their situation given the evidence she observed daily. Su Zhongkan, Su Yunfang, Xi Anzhi, Yu Daxian, and others moved through the household with the confident swagger of victors preparing to claim their prizes, making no effort to conceal their anticipation of the coming power transfer that would reshape the entire family hierarchy.

Under these increasingly oppressive circumstances, Su Tan'er maintained her daily routine of early departures and late returns, showing visible exhaustion that her occasional moments of brightness and cheerfulness seemed to represent the kind of resigned acceptance that comes with recognizing the inevitability of defeat rather than genuine optimism about future prospects. In this context, Ning Yi's apparent leisure and lack of visible concern became even more irritating to Su Danhong, prompting occasional sarcastic comments that he received with mild puzzlement rather than appropriate shame.

During one such exchange, after Su Danhong had delivered a particularly pointed criticism, Ning Yi suddenly announced: "I was just thinking..."

"About what?" she responded suspiciously, prepared for either excuse or deflection.

"Why your nephew isn't named Su Catalyst..."² he mused with apparent seriousness, though his expression suggested some private amusement that she couldn't interpret.

"Eh..." Su Danhong stared at him blankly for several moments, completely mystified by this random observation. "My husband's family name isn't Su... what's wrong with that name? I think it's quite nice—if you like it, you could use it for your first son with Tan'er..."

For the first time since her arrival, Ning Yi appeared genuinely deflated, sighing heavily before retreating with obviously dampened spirits, leaving Su Danhong to puzzle over their strange conversation without understanding why her reasonable response had produced such dramatic disappointment.

The truth was that Ning Yi had previously entertained himself by considering potential names for his future children, reasoning that since he'd married into the family, he could consider daughters named Su Danhong and sons named Su Catalyst as amusing wordplay. Su Danhong's excessively serious response to his casual testing of this private joke had backfired spectacularly when he realized mid-sentence that her husband hadn't married into her family, making his attempted humor not just unsuccessful but actively embarrassing, leaving him temporarily demoralized by his own poorly considered wit.

Five days, four days, three days, two days... time moved through this charged atmosphere with inexorable progression toward the clan assembly that would determine the family's future power structure. The twenty-fourth day of the tenth lunar month dawned with particularly beautiful morning light filtered through mist that seemed to soften the world's edges while maintaining perfect clarity about the decisive nature of the approaching confrontation, occurring just one day before the Wu family's first delivery deadline for their precious golden brocade contracts.

The morning's atmosphere throughout the Su family residence was extraordinary, charged with the certainty of fundamental change. A sense of transformation and historical significance permeated the household so intensely that even ordinary actions felt momentous as family members and associates positioned themselves for the coming redistribution of power: Su Yunfang drew a deep, savoring breath as he opened his chamber door for what he anticipated would be his last time as a subordinate; in an adjacent courtyard, Manager Yu Daxian gazed eastward with satisfaction; and Su Zhongkan, who had been watching the mist shift since before dawn, received a respectful "Second Master, good morning" from a passing Xi Anzhi.

Today would bring warfare—a conflict they considered already won—or more precisely, tonight would bring the formal confirmation of victory and the systematic harvesting of power that had been building toward this moment through months of careful planning and positioning.

Beyond the Su family compound, numerous other interested parties maintained their own surveillance of the unfolding situation, with the Xue family brothers gathering for early morning consultations while Xue Yan stood beneath his roof eaves gazing toward the Su residence, sharing confident smiles with his siblings before patting his younger brother's shoulder in anticipation of their coming success.

"Tonight I'll host dinner at that new Moon Fragrance Tower on Persimmon Street," he announced, choosing a location strategically close to the Su residence for maximum symbolic impact. "The Su family situation will reach its conclusion tonight." Similar conversations were occurring throughout Jiangning's textile community as various merchant families prepared to observe or participate in the resolution of conflicts that would reshape the industry's balance of power for years to come.

While the Su residence buzzed with momentous preparations, Ning Yi was absent. For him, this day held no more political significance than any other; it was simply another morning for his habitual run through the misty streets. His route carried him far from the family compound, past the riverside building where he exchanged friendly greetings with the beautiful, serene woman at the entrance before continuing his circuit, already planning to return for their customary conversation once his physical training was complete.

"You'll be busy today, won't you?" the woman asked with gentle concern as she served him tea during his subsequent visit, her awareness of external events highlighting his deliberate detachment from family politics. "Your household is holding its major assembly today."

"It's a clan meeting," Ning Yi replied with shameless contentment, clearly enjoying his exclusion from the proceedings. "Since I married into the family, I can't participate. So it has nothing to do with me."

Those fools could hold their meeting without him... how delightful...

¹ **"Settling Wind Waves" (定风波)**: A classical ci tune pattern, most famously used by Song Dynasty poet Su Shi in a poem about maintaining inner peace, resilience, and detachment in the face of life's political and personal storms. For a scholar to write a new poem under this classic title is a significant declaration of his own philosophical mindset.

² **"Su Catalyst" Wordplay**: This is a complex pun in the original Chinese. The cousin's name, Su Danhong (苏丹红), is also the name of a chemical dye, "Sudan Red." Ning Yi's suggested name, "Su Catalyst" (苏化剂), is a play on words referencing another chemical agent. The joke connects their family's textile/dye business with his own anachronistic scientific knowledge.
