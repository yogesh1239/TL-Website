After the city gates closed, Old Qin had been staying home most of the time, rarely venturing out. Old friends like Kang Xian would occasionally drop by for a visit, though these gatherings couldn't match the frequency of their former chess games. When Ning Yi and Nie Yunzhu arrived that afternoon, they were welcomed into the living room for brief pleasantries before Ning Yi strolled with Old Qin through the courtyard outside the study, talking as they walked. Meanwhile, Yun'niang and Madam Qin called Nie Yunzhu over - they'd apparently heard about her situation and were showing warm concern with genuine affection.

The idea of having Nie Yunzhu acknowledge Old Qin as her adoptive father¹ had only been floated by Ning Yi previously. Since Old Qin and Nie Yunzhu hadn't formally discussed it themselves, it fell to Ning Yi to broach the subject now.

Ning Yi could hardly claim he and Nie Yunzhu were unconnected to the problems that had arisen from this matter, though he certainly didn't think Nie Yunzhu bore any responsibility. The situation defied simple judgments of right or wrong, but now that it had happened, the proper course was to deal with it cleanly without troubling others. Fortunately, Old Qin was a perceptive man. Once Ning Yi explained the incident at Swallow Jade Tower, he understood both what was being said and why it was being brought up.

Yet after lengthy contemplation, he didn't immediately reveal his position, instead shifting the topic: "This year's floods upstream have reached a scale unseen in decades, yet here in Jiangning, despite the closed gates, things are actually calmer than in previous years. Does Liheng know why?" Old Qin paused before continuing. "In Jiangzhou, though the disaster is severe, they've already organized and sheltered over two hundred thousand homeless refugees, with numbers still rising. Yet reportedly they've maintained order without any outbreak of epidemic. Similarly, in Hedong Circuit where Fenzhou and Jinzhou suffered from the Yellow River breach², and in Langzhou and Guizhou on this side, they're all properly managing the aftermath and resettlement. In past years, epidemics would likely have started spreading uncontrollably by now. This year there are epidemics too, but well-ordered prefectures have contained them, preventing continuous spread..."

"Oh," Ning Yi nodded as Old Qin brought this up, realizing that with the city gates closed, outside news had come slowly and he hadn't paid much attention. Only now did he learn about these developments beyond Jiangning.

"Jiangzhou, Fenzhou, Jinzhou, Langzhou, Guizhou and other places have mostly adopted or referenced Liheng's methods. Though the methods seemed simple, the results were excellent. I've been pondering the underlying principles recently. But regardless, hundreds of thousands have benefited because of Liheng, yet today Liheng comes to discuss trivial matters of reputation with me..."

Old Qin smiled, but Ning Yi shook his head with a smile of his own. "One matter is one matter³. Originally I was taking advantage - if you don't mind such details, Old Qin, agreeing would be a favor while refusing would be reasonable. Once that favor exists, pushing further would be improper. You might not care, Old Qin, but I can't treat it as my due - that's the proper way to conduct oneself. This situation defies easy judgment of right or wrong, but reality remains reality. With various complications arising, adding more trouble would be wrong. The key point isn't that I feel bad about it, but that Yunzhu feels bad..."

Old Qin nodded but said nothing more until much later, when they'd set up a chess board in the study and the old man finally spoke: "Some days ago, I heard Lord Ming mention your conversation with Li Pin. Have you seen Mingyun recently, Liheng?"

"Been pretty busy lately," Ning Yi shook his head, "but he sent a pair of eccentric siblings to find me as students. Heh, maybe it's better not meeting - Brother Lu says he might give me a scolding when we do..."

"Heh, those would be Zhou Yong's two children - talented material, though limited by their status. They probably won't accomplish much in the future." Old Qin smiled, picking up a chess piece before pausing. "It was actually because of Liheng's theories that Mingyun and I discussed matters for several days. Later I heard about the Su family situation - Mingyun explained it rather complexly. Does Liheng have it under control?"

"Should be able to handle it," Ning Yi answered casually, not having taken the matter to heart, prompting Old Qin to study him before finally placing his piece. "Good then. Since that Li Pin is your close friend and, from what Mingyun says, quite talented, I could write him a letter of introduction when he goes to the capital."

"Then I'll thank you on Dexin's behalf." Ning Yi smiled. "By the way, that Vice Minister of Personnel⁴ Fu Ying - he wasn't one of your people before, was he?"

"Nonsense from you, boy..." Old Qin laughed and scolded, then sighed. "I'd already resigned when Li Pin passed the examination, but Fu Ying was indeed someone I promoted. His temperament leans somewhat extreme, but his work is competent. In certain matters, factional behavior⁵ is common at court - I can't control everything. From what Mingyun said, Li Pin's policy essay happened to oppose Fu Ying's salary increase proposal with rather fierce language. That's how scholars are - writing that doesn't shock serves no purpose. Who knew Fu Ying would react so strongly? Probably some political enemies mocked him to his face. Heh, these matters..."

Throughout their conversation, Qin Siyuan never once mentioned Ning Yi's discussion with Li Pin. They played their chess game while chatting about trivial matters and situations in other regions. By the time Ning Yi and Nie Yunzhu took their leave, evening approached. Neither party mentioned their stance on the "adoptive daughter" arrangement.

"Liheng... did you bring it up?" Nie Yunzhu asked softly on their way back to the riverside cottage.

"I did," Ning Yi nodded, "but he didn't nod or shake his head."

"Oh?"

"Heh, were Madam Qin and the others nice to you?"

"Mm, very nice." Yunzhu smiled and nodded. "I just worry about causing them trouble in return."

"Just visit as relatives going forward - no need to formalize anything specific. Given time, things will naturally fall into place⁶. They're all good people; even just being friends would work."

"...Mm." Yunzhu considered this and nodded. "Aunt Yun invited me to go shopping with her tomorrow and asked me to bring Jin'er along."

"That's nice."

After seeing Yunzhu home, Ning Yi was preparing to leave when she called out to him: "Liheng, about the Su family matter..." She looked at him, searching for words before finding them. "You'll definitely handle it well."

Ning Yi paused, then smiled. "Don't worry."

He arrived home just in time for dinner.

Time gradually flowed into August - a month both serious and chaotic yet seemingly as calm as ever. Apart from those truly thoughtful players with real vision, perhaps few could clearly perceive what was transpiring in Jiangning's textile industry that month, or trace the trajectories of those surging undercurrents.

The city gates remained closed, but life continued its usual course. Each day appeared identical to those before - people worked, people lived their lives. The pleasure quarters still resonated with nightly songs while refugees inside and outside the city grew increasingly desperate. Without several outside prefectures employing new disaster control methods to relieve pressure here, the oppressive atmosphere would likely weigh even heavier. Of course, even if it did, such weight would only register at the common people's level.

The Imperial Textile Bureau's imperial merchant⁷ appointments would first surface in late August with a textile guild⁸ gathering, ostensibly celebrating effective disaster relief, where interested merchants would present cloth to the imperial family. Though the decision had been made, news circulated only privately. To properly celebrate effective disaster relief, merchants would naturally need to demonstrate practical action - distributing adequate porridge and shouldering part of the government's burden.

The merchant houses that had previously held imperial merchant status naturally refused to relinquish their positions, while the Su, Xue, and Wu families' expressed interest had also stirred various medium-sized merchants, churning Jiangning's textile industry into muddy waters. Among them all, despite Su Boyong's paralysis and Su Tan'er's confinement to bed, the Su family still displayed the strongest momentum. At July's end, once Su Boyong's condition stabilized and became public knowledge, Old Master Su's tireless efforts and extensive connections finally bore fruit. The assassin Chen Er who had attacked Su Boyong confessed he'd indeed been instructed to target Su Boyong - the claim that the Su family had killed his wife and children was pure fabrication.

Who stood behind Chen Er remained undiscoverable, as he himself didn't know. But with their tarnished reputation now cleared, the Su family's chances of securing imperial merchant status had undoubtedly improved. The First Branch's shopkeepers and stewards found their morale greatly boosted while the Second and Third Branches maintained relative silence. Even if the Su family had been proven to have driven a family to death, the impact on their external business would have been limited - it was the imperial merchant status that would have suffered most. Now Old Master was actually paving the way for that very status. Could the Su family's helm truly pass to Su Tan'er?

Amid the chaotic situation, no one could clearly foresee what late August would bring. The Second and Third Branches appeared calm while the Xue family, Wu family, and other merchants pursued imperial merchant status through their own methods - negotiating deals, cultivating connections. Whatever conspiracies and calculations lurked beneath remained hidden from surface view. During this period, Ning Yi began integrating into Jiangning's textile community in his role as the Su family First Branch's temporary helmsman.

He attended various social functions⁹ and naturally encountered new people - obligations that his scholar's status had previously exempted him from, but with Su Tan'er bedridden, these social duties now fell to him. Most significant during this period was the textile guild gathering at July's end - a monthly assembly. Jiangning's textile trade maintained its own guild, headed by Wu Chenghou of the Wu family, currently the city's cloth industry leader.

During this time, Ning Yi met Wu Qihao and Wu Qilong's father, Wu Chenghou. As guild head¹⁰, he presented himself as a gentle yet authoritative middle-aged man who specifically sought out Ning Yi for an extended conversation. "We're all in the same trade - competitors, yes, but also good teachers and friends to one another. It's always been our way that when any household's goods fail to arrive on time, others extend helping hands. That's what friendship means. I've long heard of Nephew Liheng's talents. In this imperial merchant matter, the Su family holds quite favorable odds. Whatever words from the Xue family - nephew needn't take them to heart..."

He likely spoke thus because the Xue and Su families nursed longstanding grievances, with the Wu family habitually mediating between them according to Chan'er and Juan'er. When meeting Xue Jin and Xue Yan's father Xue Sheng this time, the man was indeed somewhat cool, though Xue Yan's attitude toward Ning Yi proved cordial - he specifically invited Ning Yi to dine as an apology for their previous incident.

There were also Chen Dixin of the Chen family, Lü Tianhai of the Lü family, and numerous others. After nearly a month's time, Ning Yi had grasped the general outline of Jiangning's textile industry, while these textile merchants had probably formed their own simple understanding of him.

He certainly possessed scholarly talent - being the top talent and all - but a scholar entering commerce obviously seemed out of place. Though his social appearances were limited, he spoke with grace and bearing yet couldn't shed his scholarly air. With the Su family in difficulty, this live-in son-in-law clearly wanted to help, but inexperience remained inexperience. After a full month, he'd actually accomplished nothing.

In fact, following the mix-up with Yu Hefang, he'd only managed two achievements: first was closing a deal - originally a foregone conclusion anyway, but since Ning Yi signed it, the credit naturally fell to him. There wasn't much to discuss about this, though it counted as something. The second came after he'd racked his brains to implement reforms at one of their shops.

At the time, Ning Yi had appeared quite confident to observers. After days of intense thought, he'd formulated new protocols for one shop's staff to trial. He spent three days training those shop assistants - they would greet entering customers with "Welcome," then employ standardized terminology laden with seemingly professional terms. But this reform lasted only three days before scaring away numerous customers who found the experience uncomfortable.

Thus this scholarly reform failed, becoming a joke throughout Jiangning's textile circles that seemed to deliver such a blow to Ning Yi that thereafter, beyond daily inspections, he made no further moves.

During this period he also met with He Fang, though they discussed imperial merchant matters little. He accompanied various shopkeepers in drumming up business and meeting Imperial Textile Bureau officials, but without significant effect. Those who'd previously suspected he wasn't so simple - like Xue Jin - lost interest after twenty-odd days, for the reason was simple: this was exactly how a scholar entering commerce should perform.

In the imperial merchant matter, this fellow could play no meaningful role - perhaps he merely served as a figurehead. Yet afterward, no one relaxed their vigilance, for the Su family's shopkeepers had continued operating beneath Ning Yi's performance, pushing their claim for imperial merchant status to unprecedented heights.

No conspiracies or machinations appeared during this period, as the Su family had been advancing their imperial merchant candidacy through completely open and straightforward means. Neither the Xue nor Wu families could counter such tactics, for fundamentally, the Su family had prepared for years while they hadn't. With weak foundations, they could only lag behind, at least superficially.

During this time, the siblings Zhou Pei and Zhou Junwu often appeared at Su family cloth shops waiting for Ning Yi, gradually developing a rather peculiar pattern of interaction as the seemingly calm, dull, tense yet monotonous August gradually approached its end. Dark undercurrents accumulated beneath this ordinary surface, and while none knew then that the coming days and subsequent month would completely transform Jiangning's textile industry structure, perhaps many already sensed change coming but had simply guessed the wrong direction.

Ning Yi merely watched with casual boredom. For him, life held several interesting elements, but this tableau of various lives displayed throughout the cloth shops hadn't truly penetrated his deeper consciousness. He would occasionally cast a casual glance before turning his attention elsewhere.

Whatever was meant to happen would simply unfold in its own time.
